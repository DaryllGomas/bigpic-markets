# Critical Mineral Processing & Refining: The Strategic Chokepoint

## Executive Summary

If mining is the foundation of the critical minerals supply chain, processing and refining is the strategic chokepoint where geopolitical leverage is most concentrated. China controls approximately 90% of global rare earth refining capacity, including a near-total monopoly on heavy rare earth element (HREE) separation. This midstream dominance — far more consequential than mining share — gives Beijing extraordinary leverage over defense systems, electric vehicles, wind turbines, and consumer electronics. Western nations are now spending tens of billions to build alternative processing capacity, but the timeline to meaningful independence stretches to 2030-2035 at the earliest. This report analyzes China's processing dominance, Western reshoring efforts, government strategy, recycling innovation, separation technology breakthroughs, and the critical capacity gap that defines the investment landscape.

## 1. China's Processing Dominance

### 1.1 The Scale of Control

China's grip on rare earth processing is substantially tighter than its control of mining. While China accounts for roughly 60-70% of global rare earth mining output, it controls approximately 85-90% of all downstream processing and refining. The International Energy Agency identifies China as the dominant refiner for 19 of the 20 critical minerals it tracks, with an average market share of approximately 70% across all minerals. For rare earths specifically, China's processing share approaches 90%.

The asymmetry between mining and processing share is the critical insight. Countries like the United States, Australia, and Myanmar mine significant rare earth concentrates, but nearly all of that material is shipped to China for separation, oxide-to-metal conversion, and magnet manufacturing. China is not merely a miner — it is the world's refinery.

**China's Rare Earth Supply Chain Control (2025)**

| Supply Chain Stage | China's Share | Next Largest Player |
|---|---|---|
| Mining (REO equivalent) | ~60-70% | Myanmar (~12%) |
| Separation/Refining | ~85-90% | Malaysia (Lynas, ~8%) |
| Oxide-to-Metal Conversion | ~90%+ | Estonia (Neo, <3%) |
| NdFeB Magnet Manufacturing | ~92% | Japan (~5%) |
| Heavy REE (Dy, Tb) Processing | ~98% | None at scale |

### 1.2 How China Built Dominance (1990s-Present)

China's rare earth dominance was not accidental — it was the product of deliberate, decades-long industrial policy. In the early 1990s, the United States was the world's largest rare earth producer through the Mountain Pass mine in California. China's strategy unfolded in phases:

**Phase 1 — Strategic Underpricing (1990-2002):** China flooded global markets with low-cost rare earth oxides, exploiting cheap labor, minimal environmental regulation, and state subsidies. Western producers, unable to compete on price, shuttered operations. Mountain Pass ceased rare earth processing in 1998 and closed mining in 2002.

**Phase 2 — Vertical Integration (2002-2015):** With Western competitors eliminated, China invested heavily in midstream and downstream capabilities. State-directed consolidation created national champions: China Northern Rare Earth Group and China Southern Rare Earth Group (later merged into China Rare Earth Group in 2022). These entities control the full supply chain from mine to separated oxides to metals to magnets to finished components.

**Phase 3 — Export Controls as Leverage (2010-Present):** China's 2010 export quota restrictions during a maritime dispute with Japan demonstrated the weaponization potential of rare earth dominance. Though the WTO ruled against the quotas in 2014, China shifted to more sophisticated tools — processing technology export bans, environmental inspections to control output, and strategic stockpiling.

**Phase 4 — Technology Lockdown (2023-Present):** In 2023-2025, China escalated by imposing export controls on gallium, germanium, and antimony, followed by controls on rare earth processing technology and equipment. The October 2025 restrictions specifically targeted medium and heavy rare earth elements — samarium, gadolinium, terbium, dysprosium, lutetium, scandium, and yttrium — along with their metals, oxides, alloys, and magnet materials. While some controls were temporarily suspended in November 2025 following trade negotiations, the underlying regulatory architecture remains intact.

### 1.3 China's Structural Advantages

China's processing dominance rests on several structural pillars that are extraordinarily difficult to replicate:

- **Integrated supply chains:** Chinese rare earth companies operate mine-to-magnet operations with co-located processing facilities, minimizing logistics costs and enabling rapid iteration
- **Decades of process optimization:** Approximately 70% of rare earth separation patents are held by Chinese entities, reflecting a knowledge base built over 30+ years of continuous operation
- **Scale economies:** Typical Chinese processing facilities operate at 35,000 tonnes per year, compared to 2,000-5,000 tonnes for Western facilities
- **Regulatory tolerance:** Chinese operations have historically operated with lower environmental and labor standards, though this is tightening domestically
- **State financial backing:** Chinese processors benefit from subsidized electricity, cheap capital, and strategic mineral reserves managed by state agencies

## 2. Western Reshoring Efforts

### 2.1 MP Materials (NYSE: MP) — America's Flagship

**Market Cap:** ~$10.0 billion (Feb 2026)
**Headquarters:** Las Vegas, NV
**Key Asset:** Mountain Pass Mine & Processing Facility, CA

MP Materials operates the only active rare earth mine and processing facility in the United States at Mountain Pass, California. The company has pursued an ambitious mine-to-magnet strategy that accelerated dramatically in 2025.

**Mountain Pass Operations:** The Materials segment produces rare earth concentrate (REO) from the Mountain Pass deposit, which contains one of the highest-grade rare earth ore bodies in the world. In Q3 2025, the company reported record production output.

**Fort Worth Magnetics Facility:** MP's Independence facility in Fort Worth, Texas, is designed to manufacture sintered NdFeB magnets. The smaller initial facility began commissioning with approximately 1,000 metric tons of annual capacity, with production ramping through 2026.

**DOD Partnership — The Transformational Deal:** In July 2025, MP Materials announced a landmark public-private partnership with the Department of Defense that fundamentally changed the company's trajectory:

| Deal Component | Details |
|---|---|
| DOD Equity Investment | $400M in convertible preferred stock + warrants |
| DOD Procurement Commitment | 7,000 MT/year of magnets for 10 years |
| J.P. Morgan/Goldman Sachs Financing | $1.0B construction loan for 10-X Facility |
| DOD Heavy REE Loan | $150M for heavy rare earth separation at Mountain Pass |
| 10-X Facility Capacity | 10,000 MT/year magnets (commissioning ~2028) |

This deal made the U.S. Department of Defense MP Materials' largest shareholder, marking a historic shift in American rare earth policy from encouragement to direct ownership. The 10-X facility, when complete, would make the United States a significant magnet producer for the first time in decades.

### 2.2 Lynas Rare Earths (ASX: LYC / OTC: LYSCF) — The Non-Chinese Giant

**Market Cap:** ~$10.4 billion (Jan 2026)
**Headquarters:** Perth, Australia
**Revenue (2025):** A$556.5 million

Lynas is the largest rare earth producer outside China, operating the Mt Weld mine in Western Australia and the Lynas Advanced Materials Plant (LAMP) in Gebeng, Malaysia. The Malaysian facility is one of only two industrial-scale rare earth separation plants outside China (the other being Neo's Silmet in Estonia).

**LAMP Facility (Malaysia):** Processes rare earth concentrate into separated light rare earth oxides (NdPr, lanthanum, cerium). Lynas has operated this facility since 2012, making it the most experienced non-Chinese rare earth processor.

**Texas Heavy Rare Earth Facility — Uncertain Future:** The U.S. Department of Defense awarded Lynas approximately $258 million (up from an initial $120 million in 2022) to build a heavy rare earth separation facility in Seadrift, Texas. Upon completion, this facility was to be the only scale producer of separated heavy rare earths (dysprosium, terbium) outside China.

However, the project has stalled. CEO Amanda Lacaze warned in mid-2025 that the facility "might not proceed" due to failed offtake negotiations with the DOD. The Trump administration's "America First" policy appeared to favor domestic rival MP Materials, which received the multi-billion-dollar DOD partnership while Lynas's project languished. As of early 2026, the Texas facility's future remains in limbo — a significant setback for Western heavy rare earth independence.

### 2.3 USA Rare Earth (NASDAQ: USAR)

**Headquarters:** Wheat Ridge, CO
**Key Facility:** Stillwater, Oklahoma (310,000+ sq ft)

USA Rare Earth is building a sintered NdFeB magnet manufacturing facility in Stillwater, Oklahoma, which would be the largest facility outside China with metal-and-alloy-making and strip-casting capability. In January 2025, the company produced its first batch of sintered NdFeB magnets at its Stillwater lab.

**Federal Funding:** In January 2026, the Trump Administration announced a $1.6 billion debt-and-equity investment, acquiring a 10% stake in the company. This includes $277 million in direct federal funding and a proposed $1.3 billion senior secured loan through the U.S. Department of Commerce and DOE under the CHIPS and Science Act. Commercial production of the initial 1,200-ton/year line is planned for the first half of 2026, with the facility eventually scaling to 5,000 MT/year.

**Round Top Deposit:** USA Rare Earth also holds the Round Top rhyolite deposit in Texas, containing 16 of 17 rare earth elements plus lithium. In December 2025, the company accelerated its commercial production timeline for Round Top by two years.

### 2.4 Neo Performance Materials (TSX: NEO.TO)

**Headquarters:** Toronto, Canada
**Key Assets:** Silmet (Estonia), Narva magnet plant (Estonia)

Neo operates the Silmet rare earth separation facility in Sillamae, Estonia — the only industrial-scale rare earth separation plant in the Western hemisphere outside Asia. Silmet processes approximately 2,000 tonnes per year of light rare earth concentrates into separated oxides.

**Narva Magnet Facility:** In September 2025, Neo held the grand opening of its new NdFeB magnet manufacturing facility in Narva, Estonia — Europe's first rare earth permanent magnet plant. Production began in May 2025, with mass production expected to begin later in 2026. The factory was described as the fastest-built and most competitive plant of its kind.

**Heavy Rare Earth Expansion:** Neo is progressing a heavy rare earth pilot line at Silmet to produce dysprosium and terbium, which would supply the Narva magnet facility during its ramp-up phase.

### 2.5 Ucore Rare Metals (TSXV: UCU)

**Headquarters:** Halifax, Nova Scotia
**Key Technology:** RapidSX separation
**Key Asset:** Bokan-Dotson Ridge (Alaska)

Ucore's investment thesis centers on its proprietary RapidSX separation technology, developed through subsidiary Innovation Metals Corp. RapidSX is an advanced column-based solvent extraction technique that achieves at least 3x greater efficiency than conventional solvent extraction, with shorter processing times and lower construction and operating costs.

**Demonstration Success:** Since December 2023, Ucore has operated a 52-stage RapidSX demonstration plant in Kingston, Ontario, completing approximately 5,700 hours of automated, simulated 24-hour commercial production.

**Louisiana Strategic Metals Complex:** Full commercial deployment is underway, with installation of RapidSX Machine #1 targeted for mid-2026. Management plans 2-3 modular operating lines by mid-2026 and seven lines by end of 2026.

**Government Support:** Ucore received a $22.4 million U.S. Department of Defense Other Transaction Agreement and a DPAS DO-B8 priority rating for purchase orders.

### 2.6 European Reshoring

**Solvay (EURONEXT: SOLB) — La Rochelle, France:** Solvay inaugurated its rare earth production line for permanent magnets at its La Rochelle facility, one of the few non-Chinese facilities capable of complex rare earth separation. Current capabilities focus on NdPr and samarium oxide production, with dysprosium-terbium processing scheduled to begin in 2026. Solvay has signed strategic supply agreements with Permag (US) and Less Common Metals (UK) to create a Western supply chain.

**REEtec (Norway):** REEtec has developed a proprietary chemical separation process at its Herøya facility, producing 720 tonnes/year of neodymium and praseodymium — covering approximately 5% of European demand. The process claims 90% lower CO2 emissions than competitors. LKAB (Sweden's state mining company) became the largest shareholder, with plans for a second plant in 2026-2027 fed by LKAB's mining operations.

## 3. U.S. Government Strategy for Critical Minerals

### 3.1 Department of Defense Investments

The DOD has emerged as the single most important driver of Western rare earth processing investment through multiple channels:

**Direct Equity and Procurement:**
- $400M convertible preferred stock investment in MP Materials + 7,000 MT/year, 10-year procurement commitment (July 2025)
- 10% equity stake in USA Rare Earth via $1.6B debt-and-equity package (January 2026)
- $258M allocated to Lynas for Texas heavy rare earth facility (though project is stalled)
- $22.4M Other Transaction Agreement with Ucore

**Office of Strategic Capital (OSC):** The OSC announced its first loan through its DOD agreement with MP Materials, providing $150 million for heavy rare earth separation capabilities. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act allocated $500 million to the DOD Credit Program Account under the OSC for loans, loan guarantees, and technical assistance to critical mineral industries.

**Defense Production Act Title III:** The Act provides authority for the President to incentivize domestic production of critical materials. Congress provided $1 billion for DPA financing through September 2027 through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.

### 3.2 National Defense Stockpile Modernization

Congress appropriated $2 billion for fiscal year 2025 to expand the National Defense Stockpile through critical minerals purchases. The DOD announced intent to procure up to $1 billion in stockpile materials, with the Defense Logistics Agency issuing Requests for Information covering minerals with acute supply chain vulnerabilities — including samarium, dysprosium, terbium, scandium, tungsten, and graphite. An additional $5 billion was allocated to the Industrial Base Fund to support critical minerals supply chains. Federal stockpiling efforts are expected to accelerate through 2026 and beyond.

### 3.3 DOE Loan Programs and Grants

The Department of Energy has deployed nearly $1 billion in critical minerals funding:

| Program | Amount | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Processing & Battery Manufacturing | $500M | Midstream capacity |
| Industrial Byproduct Recovery | $250M | Secondary source extraction |
| Rare Earth Supply Chain Enhancement | $135M | Full supply chain support |
| ARPA-E RECOVER Program | $40M | Critical metals from wastewater |
| Lithium Americas (LPO) | $2.2B loan | Thacker Pass lithium |
| Syrah Resources (LPO) | $98M | Natural graphite processing |

### 3.4 IRA Section 45X — Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit

The Inflation Reduction Act's Section 45X credit provides a 10% production tax credit on costs incurred for processing 50 eligible critical minerals within the United States, including all commercially relevant rare earth elements (neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, terbium, samarium, etc.), plus lithium, cobalt, nickel, graphite, and others.

However, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025 modified the credit by increasing domestic content requirements, applying "prohibited foreign entity" restrictions (targeting Chinese-owned or controlled entities), and phasing out certain wind energy provisions by December 31, 2027. The critical minerals provisions remain intact, providing ongoing incentive for domestic processing.

### 3.5 Executive Orders and Policy Evolution

**Biden Era (2021-2025):** Executive Order 14017 (February 2021) directed a 100-day supply chain review that identified critical mineral processing as a top vulnerability. Subsequent actions included the Critical Minerals Mining Reform Act, expansion of the Defense Production Act, and large DOE funding allocations.

**Trump Era (2025-Present):** The current administration has intensified critical minerals policy with an "America First" orientation, favoring domestic producers over foreign allies. Key actions include the DOD's direct equity investments in MP Materials and USA Rare Earth, expanded National Defense Stockpile funding, and the 2026 Critical Minerals Ministerial convened by the State Department (February 2026). The approach has created tension with allied producers like Lynas, whose Texas project stalled amid the pivot to domestic companies.

## 4. Recycling & Urban Mining

### 4.1 The Circular Economy Opportunity

Recycling represents a critical pathway to reducing dependence on primary rare earth mining and Chinese processing. End-of-life permanent magnets from wind turbines, EV motors, hard drives, and consumer electronics contain high concentrations of NdFeB alloy with embedded dysprosium and terbium — the most supply-constrained elements. Current recycling rates for rare earths remain below 1% globally, representing an enormous untapped resource.

### 4.2 Redwood Materials (Private)

**Founder:** JB Straubel (Tesla co-founder/former CTO)
**Headquarters:** Carson City, Nevada
**Valuation:** Estimated $5-6 billion (private)

Redwood Materials has emerged as the leading battery recycling company in North America, with a dual strategy of recycling lithium-ion batteries and remanufacturing critical battery components.

**Facilities:**
- 175-acre recycling facility in Carson City, Nevada — produces anode copper foil
- 600-acre facility in Charleston, South Carolina ($3.5B investment) — cathode active materials and anode copper foil remanufacturing
- Capability to recover 20,000 metric tons of critical minerals annually

**Production Goals:** By 2025, the company targeted 100 GWh of production capacity (sufficient for ~1 million EVs), scaling to 500 GWh by 2030 (5 million EVs). While primarily focused on lithium, nickel, and cobalt recovery, the company's processes also recover rare earth elements from motor scrap.

### 4.3 Li-Cycle / Glencore (Rochester Hub)

Li-Cycle pioneered the Spoke & Hub recycling model for lithium-ion batteries, where decentralized "Spoke" facilities shred batteries into black mass (recycled battery feedstock), which is then shipped to centralized "Hub" facilities for hydrometallurgical recovery of battery-grade lithium, nickel, and cobalt.

**Rochester Hub Status:** Construction of the Rochester Hub — designed as the first commercial hydrometallurgical battery resource recovery facility in North America — was paused in October 2023 when costs escalated beyond $475 million. Li-Cycle was subsequently delisted from the NYSE and acquired by Glencore (effective August 2025). Glencore's GBR HubCo has committed to resuming construction and is close to finalizing a $475 million DOE loan to restart operations at Eastman Business Park in Rochester, NY.

**Spoke Operations:** Three operational Spoke facilities in Rochester (NY), Gilbert (AZ), and Tuscaloosa (AL) continue to produce black mass feedstock.

### 4.4 Rare Earth Magnet Recycling

**Cyclic Materials (Private):**
The most advanced pure-play rare earth magnet recycler, Cyclic Materials uses proprietary MagCycle and REEPure processes to recover rare earth elements from end-of-life permanent magnets.

- $75 million Series C funding round completed (2025)
- Named Top 10 Climate Tech Companies to Watch by MIT Technology Review (2025)
- Mesa, Arizona: First commercial-scale Spoke facility for magnet demagnetization and processing
- Kingston, Ontario: Centre of Excellence with R&D and Hub hydrometallurgical site
- McBee, South Carolina: $82 million investment (announced January 2026) for recycling campus capable of processing 2,000 tonnes of magnet material, expanding to 6,000 tonnes/year
- Signed agreement with VACUUMSCHMELZE (VAC) to recycle 100% of magnet production byproducts from VAC's Sumter, SC manufacturing facility

**MP Materials Recycling:** MP Materials is developing recycling capabilities at Mountain Pass to supplement primary production, targeting recovery of rare earth elements from industrial scrap and end-of-life magnets.

### 4.5 Recycling Economics and Projections

The economics of rare earth recycling are improving but remain challenging:

| Factor | Current Status | 2030 Projection |
|---|---|---|
| Global REE Recycling Rate | <1% | 5-10% (est.) |
| Available Magnet Scrap (EV/Wind) | Limited (young fleet) | Growing rapidly |
| NdFeB Recycling Cost vs. Primary | 1.5-3x primary cost | Approaching parity |
| Key Economic Driver | Dy/Tb recovery value | Volume + policy support |
| Carbon Benefit vs. Primary | 70-90% lower emissions | Significant ESG premium |

The most important near-term opportunity is manufacturing scrap recycling — the 20-40% of magnet material wasted during manufacturing (grinding, cutting, rejected parts). Companies like Cyclic Materials and VAC are targeting this "low-hanging fruit" before large volumes of end-of-life EV motors become available in the 2030s.

## 5. Separation Technology

### 5.1 The Separation Challenge

Rare earth elements are notoriously difficult to separate because they have nearly identical chemical properties — they are all trivalent ions with similar ionic radii, making selective extraction extremely challenging. This is why processing is the true bottleneck, not mining.

### 5.2 Conventional Solvent Extraction (SX)

The dominant commercial technology for rare earth separation is liquid-liquid solvent extraction, using organophosphorus extractants (D2EHPA, PC88A, Cyanex 272) dissolved in organic diluents. The process requires:

- Hundreds of mixer-settler stages arranged in counter-current cascades
- Large physical footprint (major Chinese facilities span multiple hectares)
- Significant chemical consumption (hydrochloric acid, sodium hydroxide, organic solvents)
- Weeks of processing time to achieve individual element separation
- Generation of large volumes of acidic/basic wastewater and organic waste

China's cost advantage in conventional SX stems from scale (35,000+ MT/year facilities), decades of optimization, cheap labor and electricity, and tolerance for environmental externalities.

### 5.3 Next-Generation Approaches

**Continuous Ion Exchange (CIX) / Continuous Ion Chromatography (CIC):**
Column-based separation using ion exchange resins, offering higher selectivity than SX for certain element pairs. Several companies and research groups are developing CIX/CIC approaches, though commercial scale remains limited.

**Ucore RapidSX:**
Ucore's proprietary column-based solvent extraction achieves at least 3x greater efficiency than conventional SX through proprietary contactor columns and phase separators that enable efficient mixing without powered mixing tanks. The 52-stage demonstration plant in Kingston has validated the technology over 5,700+ hours of automated operation.

**REEtec Oxalic Acid Process (Norway):**
REEtec has developed a precipitation-based approach using oxalic acid chemistry that avoids the massive solvent extraction cascades. The process operates at the Heroya facility, producing 720 tonnes/year of NdPr oxide with claimed 90% reduction in CO2 emissions. LKAB's investment positions this as a cornerstone of Scandinavian rare earth processing.

**Membrane-Based Separation:**
Emerging research on supported liquid membranes and hollow fiber contactors for selective rare earth extraction. These approaches offer potential for smaller footprint and continuous operation, but remain at laboratory/pilot scale.

### 5.4 Processing Cost Comparison

| Facility Type | Separation Cost ($/kg REO) | Capital Cost | Environmental Footprint |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large Chinese SX Plant | $3-8/kg | Low (amortized) | High (but improving) |
| Western SX Plant (Lynas LAMP) | $8-15/kg | Moderate | Moderate (regulated) |
| Ucore RapidSX | $6-12/kg (projected) | Lower (modular) | Lower (smaller footprint) |
| REEtec Oxalic Acid | $10-18/kg (est.) | Moderate | Lowest (90% less CO2) |
| Recycling (Cyclic Materials) | $15-25/kg (current) | Moderate | Very Low |

### 5.5 Environmental Footprint of Processing

Rare earth processing carries significant environmental burdens that have shaped the industry's geographic concentration:

**Radioactive Waste:** Rare earth minerals (monazite, bastnaesite, xenotime) naturally contain thorium and uranium. For every ton of rare earth produced, approximately one ton of radioactive residue is generated. At China's Bayan-Obo mine, over 70,000 tonnes of radioactive thorium are stored with inadequate containment. The radioactive waste problem is a primary reason Western nations abandoned rare earth processing in the 1990s — regulatory compliance costs made Western operations uneconomic.

**Chemical Waste:** Processing generates approximately 9,600-12,000 cubic meters of waste gas and 75 cubic meters of wastewater per ton of rare earth produced. Conventional solvent extraction requires large volumes of sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, and ammonia.

**Tailings:** Improperly managed tailings ponds have caused severe environmental contamination in China, Myanmar, and Malaysia. Lynas's LAMP facility in Malaysia has faced sustained community opposition over radioactive waste storage.

**Western Regulatory Burden:** The high cost of compliant rare earth processing in Western nations — proper waste treatment, radioactive material handling, environmental monitoring — adds $5-15/kg to processing costs compared to Chinese operations, though China is progressively tightening its own environmental standards.

### 6.1 Current Western Processing Capacity vs. Demand

The gap between Western processing capacity and projected demand is the central challenge for supply chain independence.

**Current Western Rare Earth Separation Capacity (2026)**

|---|---|---|---|

| NdFeB Magnets | ~2,500 (MP + USA RE + Neo starting) | ~25,000 | -90% |

### 6.2 Projected Demand Growth

The IEA projects a 40% increase in rare earth demand by 2030, driven by EV motors, offshore wind generators, and defense applications. More bullish estimates project global rare earth oxide demand to nearly double from ~200,000 MT in 2023 to ~400,000 MT by 2030.

**Key Demand Drivers:**

|---|---|---|---|

### 6.3 Timeline to Close the Gap

Even with maximum acceleration, the IEA and industry analysts project that closing the processing gap will require 10-15 years of sustained investment:

**Phase 1 (2025-2027): Foundation**
- MP Materials Fort Worth initial 1,000 MT magnet production
- USA Rare Earth Stillwater initial commissioning
- Neo Narva magnet production ramp
- Ucore Louisiana RapidSX deployment
- Western processing share: ~10-12%

**Phase 2 (2028-2030): Scaling**
- MP Materials 10-X facility commissioning (7,000-10,000 MT magnets)
- USA Rare Earth full 5,000 MT capacity
- Lynas Texas HREE (if proceeds)
- Expanded recycling from Cyclic Materials, Redwood
- Western processing share: ~15-20%

**Phase 3 (2030-2035): Independence**
- Multiple scaled magnet facilities operating
- HREE separation at scale (MP, potentially Lynas, Ucore)
- Significant recycling volumes from first-generation EV fleet
- Western processing share target: ~25-30%

### 6.4 Investment Required

The total capital required to build meaningful Western processing independence is estimated at $15-25 billion across the full supply chain:

| Investment Category | Estimated Capital (2025-2035) |
|---|---|
| Rare Earth Separation Facilities | $3-5B |
| Magnet Manufacturing Plants | $5-8B |
| Heavy REE Processing | $2-3B |

| R&D and Workforce Development | $2-4B |
| **Total** | **$15-25B** |

Government funding committed to date (DOD, DOE, CHIPS Act) accounts for approximately $8-10 billion, meaning private capital must fill a gap of $5-15 billion. The Section 45X production credit, at 10% of processing costs, provides ongoing operational subsidy but does not fully close the economics gap versus Chinese producers.

### 6.5 Mineral-by-Mineral Bottleneck Analysis

**Critical Bottlenecks (Worst Processing Gaps):**
1. **Dysprosium:** No Western scale separation. 98%+ processed in China. Essential for high-temperature NdFeB magnets in EV motors and defense. If China's supply were excluded, remaining supplies would cover only 35-40% of demand.
2. **Terbium:** Same processing gap as dysprosium. Used in high-performance magnets and phosphors.
3. **Heavy REE Group (Ho, Er, Tm, Yb, Lu):** No Western processing. Small volumes but critical for specialized applications.

**Moderate Bottlenecks:**
4. **Neodymium-Praseodymium (NdPr):** Lynas LAMP and Neo Silmet provide some non-Chinese capacity, but ~80% still processed in China.
5. **Samarium:** Limited Western processing (Solvay La Rochelle beginning 2026). Essential for SmCo magnets used in defense.

**Improving Situation:**
6. **Light REE (La, Ce):** Oversupplied relative to demand; processing bottleneck is less acute.
7. **Lithium, Cobalt, Nickel:** Multiple Western processing projects underway with better economics than rare earths.

### 7.3 IEA Projections for Market Share Shift

The IEA projects that by 2030, China will still control 51% of rare earth mining and 76% of refining — down from current levels but still dominant. Under bullish scenarios with maximum Western acceleration, China's processing share could drop from ~90% to ~75% by 2028. Full diversification would require a sustained multi-decade effort.

---

## 8. Key Risks and Considerations

**Execution Risk:** Nearly every Western rare earth project has experienced delays and cost overruns. The Lynas Texas stall, Li-Cycle Rochester Hub pause, and repeated timeline extensions across the sector suggest that 2028-2030 targets may slip.

**China's Response:** China can crash rare earth prices to undermine Western investment economics, as it did in the 1990s. The temporary suspension of export controls in November 2025 may partly serve this purpose — giving Western projects a sense of reduced urgency while maintaining the architecture for future restrictions.

**Technology Gaps:** Western facilities remain 5-10 years behind Chinese operations in process optimization and yield. Approximately 70% of separation patents are held by Chinese entities, creating a knowledge deficit.

**Demand Uncertainty:** If EV adoption slows or alternative motor technologies (ferrite, wound-rotor) gain traction, rare earth demand growth could disappoint, undermining the investment case for new processing capacity.

**Policy Continuity:** Western rare earth strategy depends on sustained government support across multiple election cycles. A shift in priorities could strand partially built facilities.
