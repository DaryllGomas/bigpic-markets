## Executive Summary

The defense and aerospace sector is entering a generational supercycle unlike anything since the Reagan-era buildup. Three converging forces are driving structural, not cyclical, demand growth: NATO's rearmament from the 2% to 3.5% GDP spending target (with Europe's largest economies spending at rates not seen since the Cold War), the urgent need to replenish munitions stockpiles depleted by the Ukraine conflict, and the emergence of space defense as a fully funded operational domain through the Golden Dome initiative. Combined backlogs across the six major primes now exceed $800 billion -- representing approximately 2.5 years of aggregate revenue -- while $28.8 billion in multi-year procurement authority and landmark framework agreements for missile production provide demand visibility extending well beyond annual appropriations cycles.

The key tension in this thesis is between extraordinary demand and constrained supply. Record order books are colliding with structural labor shortages (25% of the defense workforce at or near retirement), supply chain bottlenecks in critical areas (solid rocket motors, propellants, seekers), and production ramp timelines that consistently slip. The 155mm artillery shell production goal -- a 7x increase from pre-Ukraine baselines -- has already slipped from late 2025 to mid-2026. Companies that can ramp production capacity fastest will capture disproportionate value, while those unable to execute risk margin compression and contract penalties.

Why now: The investment window is defined by three time-bound catalysts. First, the FY2026 NDAA's $28.8B multi-year procurement authority is activating a wave of factory construction and hiring that creates measurable milestones for the next 18-24 months. Second, the Golden Dome initiative is injecting $24-38 billion in near-term space defense spending that must be obligated before reconciliation authority expires. Third, the emerging defense technology companies (Anduril, Shield AI, Saronic) are approaching IPO timelines in 2026-2027, creating new public market entry points for the autonomous systems revolution. Margins are expanding -- defense tech operating margins grew 230 bps in 2025 with another 120 bps expected in 2026 -- and book-to-bill ratios above 1.0x across the sector confirm that the demand wave is still building, not cresting.

## Key Sectors

### 1. Defense Prime Contractors

The six major primes (LMT, RTX, NOC, GD, BA, LHX) hold combined backlogs exceeding $800 billion and are guiding 8-10% EPS CAGR through FY2028. Book-to-bill ratios above 1.0x across the board confirm demand continues to outpace deliveries -- RTX's Raytheon segment posted 2.27x book-to-bill in Q3 2025, signaling missile demand dramatically exceeds production capacity.

**Lockheed Martin (LMT):** Franchise programs are F-35 (191 deliveries in 2025, Lots 18-19 for 296 aircraft at $24B), PAC-3 MSE (record 600+ produced, ramping to 2,000/year by 2030), and THAAD (framework to quadruple production to 400/year). Investing $5B internally in 2026 (+35% YoY), signaling the growth opportunity justifies retaining capital vs. buybacks.

**RTX Corporation (RTX):** Largest aerospace/defense company by revenue. The $50B Patriot umbrella contract and five landmark missile framework agreements (Tomahawk >1,000/yr, AMRAAM >1,900/yr, SM-6 >500/yr) represent 2-4x production increases. GTF engine recall remains an overhang but is recovering (aircraft-on-ground down 20% from peak).

**Northrop Grumman (NOC):** Most classified-program-heavy prime. B-21 Raider in LRIP with $4.5B appropriation for production acceleration (financial impact 2027-2029). Sentinel ICBM at $140.9B (81% cost overrun) but deemed must-continue. Space segment (~$11B) is a differentiator with SDA satellite deliveries.

**General Dynamics (GD):** Unique defense+luxury jet combination. Columbia-class submarine is the Navy's top priority (first boat 60% complete, $5.1B contract). Gulfstream delivered 158 aircraft in 2025. GD-OTS dominates large-caliber ammunition but faces execution risk on 155mm UAPL lines.

**L3Harris (LHX):** Highest margins among the six primes (segment operating margin in low 16% range). Aerojet Rocketdyne integration driving 12% organic growth. Five new solid rocket motor factories backed by $1B DOD investment. Missile Solutions IPO planned H2 2026.

### 4. Space Defense

Space defense is the fastest-growing segment of national security spending, with the Space Force budget surging to ~$40B in FY2026 when reconciliation funding is included -- a 159% increase from its founding budget.

**PWSA (Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture):**

| Tranche | Satellites | Status | Key Contractors |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tranche 0 | 27 | Deployed (2023-2024) | Pathfinder |
| Tranche 1 | 158 | Deploying 2025-2027 | LMT, York Space |
| Tranche 2 | 170+ | Production/Launch ~2027 | RKLB ($515M), LHX ($919M), LMT ($890M), NOC, Sierra Space ($740M) |
| Tranche 3 | 72+ (tracking) | Awarded Dec 2025, Launch ~FY2029 | RKLB ($816M), LHX ($843M), LMT, NOC |

PWSA replaces the traditional model of a few $2-5B exquisite satellites with constellations of $40-50M spacecraft, enabling resilience through numbers (destroying 10 of 300 satellites degrades capability by ~3% vs. 50% for 2 GEO satellites), rapid replenishment (12-18 months vs. 5-7 years), and technology refresh every 2 years.

**Golden Dome:** The continental missile defense initiative with cost estimates ranging from $175B (White House) to $3.6T (AEI). FY2025-26 funding of $24.4B and $13.4B respectively, with ~$7.7B earmarked for space-based long-range kill chains. Primary beneficiaries: LMT (Next-Gen OPIR, NGI at $17.7B), NOC (GPI prime), LHX (sensors, propulsion), RTX (SM-3/SM-6, radar systems).

**SpaceX Dominance:** Starshield (183+ satellites launched for NRO), MILNET (480-satellite military SATCOM constellation), and 52% of NSSL Phase 3 launch missions ($5.9B). SpaceX concentration is both a capability enabler and a competitive risk for publicly-traded peers.

## Risk Factors

1. **Execution and production ramp risk (HIGH):** Record backlogs mean nothing if production cannot scale. GD-OTS UAPL failures, 155mm target slippage, and Sentinel's 81% cost overrun demonstrate that defense manufacturing is difficult. Labor shortages (25% at retirement age, security clearance bottleneck up 1,000% since 2014) are the binding constraint across the industrial base.

2. **Fixed-price contract losses (HIGH for BA, MODERATE for sector):** Boeing Defense has destroyed billions in shareholder value through KC-46, T-7A, and Starliner losses. While the trend is improving (-6.8% margin vs. -41.9% prior year), the fixed-price model creates asymmetric downside when programs run over budget.

3. **Political and budget risk (MODERATE):** Defense spending has strong bipartisan support but is subject to continuing resolutions (which freeze new program starts), sequestration risk, and shifting priorities between administrations. Golden Dome reconciliation funding is non-recurring -- FY2027+ requires regular appropriations.

4. **Supply chain fragility (MODERATE-HIGH):** Single-source dependencies are critical vulnerabilities. Radford AAP is the sole U.S. source for propellants. Boeing is the sole seeker supplier for PAC-3. Solid rocket motor capacity is concentrated. Specialty metals, rad-hard microelectronics, and energetic materials face structural constraints.

5. **Valuation risk in defense tech (HIGH for PLTR, MODERATE for KTOS/AVAV):** Palantir at 46-52x forward revenue and 190+ P/E prices in years of hypergrowth. Private companies (Anduril at $30.5-79.6B, Shield AI at $5.6B) face IPO pricing risk. Any deceleration in the defense AI narrative could cause significant multiple compression.

6. **SpaceX concentration risk (MODERATE):** SpaceX dominance in launch (52% of NSSL Phase 3), SATCOM (MILNET, Starshield), and satellite manufacturing creates dependency for DOD and competitive pressure for ULA, Blue Origin, and Rocket Lab. Not investable directly (private), and its dominance could compress peers' pricing power.

7. **Technology risk (MODERATE):** Hypersonic programs have long histories of test failures. Space-based interceptors remain unproven. Autonomous systems at REPLICATOR scale have never been attempted. Adversary countermeasures (EW, cyber, adversarial AI) could degrade effectiveness.

8. **Geopolitical escalation (LOW probability, HIGH impact):** A Taiwan contingency or direct NATO-Russia confrontation would validate the thesis but could disrupt supply chains, trigger emergency procurement outside normal channels, and create volatile market conditions.

9. **Sustainment cost overruns (MODERATE):** F-35 sustainment costs remain above targets ($7.5M vs. $6.8M per tail per year). Naval ship maintenance has a $2.3B deferred backlog. Rising sustainment costs could crowd out new procurement funding.

10. **Ukraine funding uncertainty (MODERATE):** A reduction or cessation of Ukraine military aid would remove a demand vector for munitions replenishment, though NATO's own rearmament requirements are now self-sustaining regardless of Ukraine outcomes.
