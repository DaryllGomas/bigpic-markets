# Commodities & Forex — Eagle Eye W21
**Week ending Friday, May 22, 2026 | Snapshot as of Sunday, May 24, 2026**

> **MACRO CONTEXT:** All commodity markets this week were dominated by a single structural shock — the ongoing U.S.-Iran war, now entering its 12th week. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 4, disrupting ~20 million barrels/day (~20% of global seaborne oil trade and significant LNG volumes), which the IEA has called "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." A potential peace deal "largely negotiated" over the weekend of May 23-24 is the most consequential geopolitical development in the commodity complex in years.

### Brent Crude
- **Friday close (May 22):** $103.54/bbl (+$0.96 on the day)
- **Intraday high (Fri):** ~$106 before sellers took control
- **Weekly change:** -5%+ for the week
- **Spread:** Brent premium to WTI ~$6.94/bbl

### Driver: Iran Peace Talks
Oil fell sharply all week as diplomatic progress on ending the U.S.-Iran war gained credibility:
- **Monday (May 18):** Trump called off imminent new strikes on Iran to allow negotiations; WTI tumbled
- **Thursday (May 21):** Secretary of State Rubio said there were "good signs" a deal was near but warned it would be "unfeasible" if Iran pursues permanent Hormuz control
- **Friday (May 22):** U.S. crude fell nearly 2% at one point to $96.35 intraday; prices partially recovered into the close
- Key sticking points remain: Iran's enriched uranium stockpile and its demand for authority to manage (toll) the Strait of Hormuz

### EIA/Inventory Data
- **Week ending May 15:** U.S. crude inventories fell 9.10 million barrels (API estimate), far exceeding the ~3.4 million barrel draw expected; the SPR saw a record weekly draw of 9.90 million barrels, bringing SPR stocks to 374.2 million barrels (lowest since July 2024)
- **Week ending May 8:** Inventories fell 2.19 million barrels (fourth straight weekly decline)
- **IEA May 2026 forecast:** Global inventories projected to fall 8.5 mb/d in Q2 2026; cumulative deficit reaching 900 mb by September 2026; IEA has released 164 mb of a planned 400 mb coordinated stock release
- U.S. crude inventories stood at ~461.6 million barrels as of the latest data, ~0.1% above the five-year seasonal average — masking the ongoing structural draw

### Natural Gas (Henry Hub)
- **Friday close (May 22):** ~$2.91–$2.93/MMBtu (sources vary slightly; -3% to -5% on the day)
- Natural gas has been more insulated from the Hormuz shock than crude, though global LNG rerouting has added complexity to European and Asian markets

### OPEC+
- **May 3 meeting:** OPEC+ (now without the UAE, which exited the cartel) agreed to a 188,000 b/d output increase from the additional voluntary adjustments pool
- **UAE departure:** The UAE — previously OPEC's third-largest producer — announced it was leaving the cartel, concluding its national interest was better served independently; a significant structural shift
- **Context:** OPEC+ output increases are largely irrelevant to near-term supply given the Hormuz blockade; Iraqi, Saudi, Kuwaiti, and UAE export routes through the Gulf are all affected by the closure

## Weekend Developments

**This section is CRITICAL this week. The biggest commodity market story of 2026 is actively developing over this weekend (May 23-24, 2026).**

### Trump Declares Iran Deal "Largely Negotiated" — Saturday, May 23, 2026

On Saturday, May 23, President Trump announced that a peace deal with Iran — including reopening the Strait of Hormuz — has been "largely negotiated" following calls with Israeli and regional allied leaders. Trump said the memorandum of understanding would be detailed "shortly."

**Key reported deal elements:**
- Iran would commit not to pursue nuclear weapons
- Iran would surrender its stockpile of highly enriched uranium (HEU) — part to be diluted, remainder transferred to a third country (potentially Russia) over a 60-day period
- The Strait of Hormuz would be reopened; Iran would give up blockade control
- A 60-day ceasefire extension would accompany the deal, during which broader negotiations on nuclear program curtailment and sanctions relief would proceed

**Key unresolved issues and Iranian counter-narrative:**
- Iran's state-linked Fars news agency described Trump's framing as "inconsistent with reality" and said wide disagreements persist on Hormuz control — Iran wants permanent authority to manage (and toll) the strait
- Iran is seeking a permanent end to the war before HEU negotiations, plus sanctions relief and war reparations
- Secretary of State Rubio characterized progress as "significant, although not final"
- No official Iranian confirmation as of Sunday morning, May 24

**Market implications if deal closes:**
- Analysts estimate an immediate $10–$20 crude oil price decline on formal Hormuz reopening announcement; some projections see Brent falling to the $80–$90 range once supply bottlenecks clear (vs. ~$103.54 close Friday)
- WTI could fall through $85 on a full deal, though damage to Gulf infrastructure and rerouting logistics mean physical supply normalization will lag the market signal by weeks-to-months
- Gold would likely decline modestly on risk-on pivot, but high bond yields/rate-hike odds provide floor
- USD would likely weaken on reduced safe-haven demand; EUR, JPY, EM currencies would benefit
- Natural gas markets (LNG) would also ease as Qatari and other Gulf LNG routes normalize

**Market implications if deal collapses:**
- Oil would likely retest the $110–$120 Brent range on renewed escalation fears
- Gold would spike back toward $4,600–$4,700 as safe-haven demand surges
- BDI would continue rising as rerouting demand persists

**Surveillance for Monday open (May 25, 2026):**
- Watch for official statements from Iranian government channels (not just Fars) confirming or denying the deal
- Watch WTI and Brent futures when electronic markets open Sunday evening for the market's immediate read on the deal's credibility
- Memorial Day holiday in the U.S. means reduced U.S. cash market liquidity on Monday

### Other Weekend Developments
- No significant OPEC+ emergency meetings announced over the weekend
- No additional geopolitical supply disruptions (beyond the ongoing Hormuz closure) identified in Africa, Russia, or the Americas
