## Fed Watch

### Leadership Transition — MAJOR EVENT THIS WEEK

**Kevin Warsh sworn in as Fed Chair on Friday, May 22.** Warsh took the oath of office from Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas in the East Room of the White House, officially succeeding Jerome Powell, who served as chair from February 2018 through May 15, 2026. The Senate confirmed Warsh on May 13, 2026, in a 54–45 vote — the most divisive Fed chair confirmation in history.

Warsh's first public remarks as chair:
> "I am not naive about the challenges facing the US economy... Inflation can be lower and growth can be strong."

He stressed the Fed's obligation to act **independently**, while noting that "Fed independence is largely up to the Fed."

**Powell's exit:** Powell ended his eight-year tenure with a notable final FOMC press conference (April 29). His last public word on inflation at that meeting: a "20-word reality check" warning that price stability remains paramount. He plans to remain on the Board of Governors but maintain a "low profile."

### FOMC Minutes — April 28–29 Meeting (Released May 20, 2026)

Released Wednesday, May 20. Key findings:

- **Rate held at 3.50–3.75%** — third consecutive hold.
- **Four dissents** — the most since 1992. Three hawkish (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan), one dovish (Miran, who wanted a 25 bps cut).
- **"A majority" of participants said a rate hike would likely become appropriate if inflation persisted above 2%.** "Many" officials called for dropping the easing bias entirely and signaling the next move could be a hike, not a cut.
- **"Two-sided" framework adopted de facto:** The committee's reaction function is now explicitly bidirectional — the bar for cuts has risen sharply, and the Fed is prepared to tighten if Iran war-driven inflation proves sustained.
- **"Almost all participants" flagged that higher energy/input costs could delay the return to target.**

**Hawkish dissenter quotes (from post-meeting statements, early May):**

- **Beth Hammack (Cleveland):** "Inflation pressures continue to be broad-based, and rising oil prices present an additional source of inflationary pressure... I see this easing bias as no longer appropriate given the outlook."
- **Neel Kashkari (Minneapolis):** "Federal funds rate increases, potentially a series of them, could be warranted even at the risk of further weakness to the labor market." Said the Strait of Hormuz closure could produce "a price shock wave much larger than is currently expected."
- **Lorie Logan (Dallas):** "It could plausibly be appropriate for the FOMC's next rate change to be either an increase or a cut... I believed the FOMC should not give forward guidance implying a bias toward rate cuts at this time."

### Current Fed Funds Rate & Meeting Schedule

- **Current target range:** 3.50%–3.75% (held since the Jan 28, 2026 meeting)
- **Next FOMC meeting:** June 16–17, 2026 (SEP meeting; Warsh's first as chair)

**Major bank outlooks:** J.P. Morgan now projects rates unchanged through year-end with the next move a 25 bps **hike** in Q3 2027.

## Liquidity

| Metric                          | Level (week of May 22, 2026) | Direction      |
|---------------------------------|------------------------------|----------------|
| ON RRP (Overnight Reverse Repo) | ~$0.97 billion               | Near-zero / depleted |
| TGA (Treasury General Account)  | ~$905 billion                | Elevated        |

**ON RRP near zero:** The overnight reverse repo facility has effectively been drained — from its peak of ~$2.6T in 2022-2023 to near zero. This removes a key buffer of "shadow liquidity" from money markets. Bank reserves remain ample for now.

**TGA elevated:** The Treasury is maintaining a large cash buffer (~$905B) ahead of potential debt ceiling/fiscal stress scenarios. A large TGA acts as a liquidity drain from the banking system — when Treasury spends it down, reserves are injected.

**Fiscal watch:** The Moody's downgrade of the US from Aaa to Aa1 (announced May 16, 2026 — one week ago) cited persistent and widening fiscal deficits and rising interest costs. The "One Big Beautiful Bill" reconciliation package advancing through Congress is projected to add ~$3.3T to the debt over the next decade. Bank of America declared "bond vigilantes have returned," with the 30-year yield hitting 5.18% intraweek — its highest since 2007.

Published Sunday morning. Key content: Bond investors are betting Warsh will prioritize inflation-fighting credibility over Trump's demands for rate cuts. Interest-rate swaps now fully price at least one 25 bps hike by year-end 2026 — a complete reversal from the multiple-cuts scenario priced just months ago when Warsh was nominated. ### 2. Fortune: "US Debt is the Elephant in the Room" (May 23)
Published Saturday. Bank of America labeled fiscal policy "the elephant in the room" and declared bond vigilantes have returned. Key data: The 30-year yield hit 5.18% on Tuesday (intraweek high), highest since 2007. CRFB estimated that if rates remain ~55 bps above CBO projections, the US debt burden worsens materially beyond already-elevated projections. Commentary frames the Moody's Aa1 downgrade (May 16) + the reconciliation bill + Iran war energy shock as a triple-layer fiscal/inflation threat.

### 3. Motley Fool: "Trump Set Fed Chair Warsh Up for Failure — Wall Street Will Pay the Price" (May 24)
Commentary arguing Warsh faces an impossible mandate — Trump publicly demands rate cuts, but the inflation/fiscal backdrop may require hikes. Market tension between political pressure and central bank credibility is the defining near-term risk for rates.

### 4. Iran War Context (geopolitical backdrop)
The ongoing US-Iran conflict — which began in late February 2026 — continues to drive the energy/inflation shock underpinning the rate-hike narrative. Brent crude near $109, WTI near $106 as of Friday's close. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. No ceasefire/deal signals over the weekend that would materially change the inflation trajectory.

**Net weekend assessment:** No new hard data or Fed policy announcements over the weekend, but the framing narrative is solidifying: the Warsh Fed is being immediately tested by a market that is pricing in the real possibility of rate hikes, not cuts, in 2026. This is a profound pivot from the start of the year.
