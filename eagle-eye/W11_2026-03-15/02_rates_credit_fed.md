Yields climbed sharply for the second consecutive week as the Iran-Hormuz oil shock continued to re-price inflation expectations. The 10Y rose ~13 bp on the week; the long end bore the brunt with 30Y approaching 5%.

**Key drivers of the weekly move:**
- **Iran oil shock / Strait of Hormuz blockade:** Oil above $100/bbl for the first time since 2022, with Brent briefly trading near $120 early in the week. Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei pledged to keep the Strait "effectively closed." This is the single largest supply disruption since the 1979 Iranian Revolution — ~15 million bpd of Gulf supply at risk.
- **CPI in-line but irrelevant to forward outlook:** February CPI came in at 2.4% YoY headline / 2.5% core (released 3/11), matching estimates. Normally dovish, but the data is backward-looking and predates the oil spike's full pass-through. Markets shrugged.
- **GDP revision shock:** Q4 2025 GDP was revised sharply lower to 0.7% annualized (from 1.4% initial estimate), stoking stagflation fears.
- **Record IG issuance:** Amazon's $37B 11-part bond sale on 3/10 helped drive a record $66B single-day IG issuance session, absorbing significant duration.

The 10Y auction on March 11 priced at 4.217%. The 30Y auction followed on March 12. Both settled on 3/16.

## Fed Watch

### FOMC March 17-18 Preview — THE Main Event

This is the most consequential FOMC meeting since the Iran war began. It is a **Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) meeting** — the first of 2026 — delivering:
- Updated dot plot (first 2026-vintage rate projections)
- Revised GDP, unemployment, and PCE inflation forecasts
- Powell's final quarterly press conference before Kevin Warsh's expected confirmation

**Rate Decision:**
- **CME FedWatch:** 96% probability of a hold at 3.50-3.75%; 4% probability of a 25 bp cut.
- Unanimous consensus: The Fed will hold. The real action is in the projections.

**Dot Plot Expectations:**
- The December 2025 SEP showed a **median of one 25 bp cut in 2026** (year-end target: 3.40%).
- The market is focused on whether the median shifts:
  - **Hawkish risk (zero cuts):** If the dot plot removes the single projected cut, it would represent an explicit acknowledgment that oil-driven inflation has delayed easing. Very bearish for risk assets.
  - **Base case (one cut maintained):** Median stays at 3.40% but with upward revisions to near-term PCE inflation (from 2.6% to ~2.8-3.0% for mid-2026) and a GDP downgrade. This is the "concerned but patient" signal.
  - **Dovish surprise (two cuts):** Unlikely given the inflation backdrop, but Governors Miran and Waller may push in this direction. Would be bullish for risk assets.
- **The stagflation test:** If the Fed simultaneously raises its inflation forecast and cuts its GDP forecast, that is a textbook stagflation signal. Markets will parse this carefully.

**SEP Forecasts to Watch (December 2025 baseline):**
- PCE Inflation: 2.6% YE 2026 (likely revised higher to 2.8-3.0%)
- Core PCE: TBD (energy pass-through to core is the key debate)
- GDP Growth: 2.3% (likely revised lower given Q4 GDP revision to 0.7%)
- Unemployment: 4.4% YE 2026

### Fed Speakers This Week

The FOMC **blackout period began March 7**, so there was no Fed commentary during Week 11. However, pre-blackout positioning was significant:

- **Carl Weinberg (High Frequency Economics):** Argued the Fed should consider a rate *hike* at the March meeting to push back against oil-shock inflation potentially reaching 3.5% by summer. An outlier view, but indicative of the hawkish tail risk.
- **Governor Christopher Waller (pre-blackout):** Continued to argue there is "little reason for the Fed to sit on its hands" if the labor market weakens. Expected to dissent for a 25 bp cut at March meeting.
- **Governor Stephen Miran (pre-blackout):** "Almost certain to dissent in a dovish direction" per Pepperstone's FOMC preview. Miran has consistently voted for more aggressive easing.
- **January meeting minutes (released 2/19):** Revealed the committee was "divided over the future path of interest rates," with some members "open to a hike" — a hawkish surprise that set the tone for this meeting.

Goldman Sachs pushed its next-cut call from June to September. The Iran oil shock has fundamentally repriced the easing path.

### Kevin Warsh Transition

Warsh is Trump's nominee to replace Powell (term expires May 15). Warsh has publicly stated rates should be lower than 3.50-3.75%, aligning with Trump's preference for lower rates. However, he will inherit a Fed that may be boxed in by oil-driven inflation. CNBC noted Warsh's view of inflation "doesn't put much emphasis on factors like oil," suggesting he may still lean toward cuts even with elevated energy prices — a potential divergence from the current committee's stance.

## Liquidity

### Treasury General Account (TGA)
- **Balance as of 3/6:** ~$833B (down from ~$849B on 2/26)
- The TGA is gradually declining from its post-tax-season peak, which is mildly liquidity-positive as Treasury draws down cash and effectively injects reserves into the banking system.
- The DTS was updated through 3/13; the exact closing balance is available on FiscalData.Treasury.gov.

### Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP)
- **Award rate:** 3.50% (as of 3/9)
- **Usage level:** The ON RRP facility has been running at very low levels throughout 2026, consistent with the drain that has been underway since mid-2023 when usage peaked above $2 trillion. Current usage is estimated in the low tens of billions — functionally near the floor. This means the "excess liquidity cushion" from the ON RRP is effectively exhausted, and any further balance sheet normalization (QT) will draw directly from bank reserves.
- The Fed ended QT in early 2025, but the reserve environment remains a focal point as war-related fiscal spending could alter the TGA trajectory.

### Bank Reserves
- With ON RRP near zero, total reserves are the binding liquidity constraint. The January 2026 Senior Loan Officer Survey (SLOOS) reported:
  - **Tighter standards** for C&I loans to firms of all sizes in Q4 2025
  - **Stronger demand** for C&I loans to large and middle-market firms
  - Banks cited "less favorable or more uncertain economic outlook" and "expected deteriorations in collateral values" as reasons for tightening
  - **2026 outlook:** Banks expect standards to remain "basically unchanged" for most categories, but expect to tighten for construction/land development loans
  - Mixed expectations for C&I credit quality; expected deterioration in residential and consumer loan categories

- **Oil prices (weekend):** Brent crude remains above $100/bbl heading into the weekend. Early Sunday futures are expected to gap higher on the no-ceasefire headlines.

### Implications for Monday / FOMC Week
- Bond futures are likely to open with a bearish bias (higher yields) on Monday given the weekend escalation. The 10Y could test 4.35-4.40% early in the week.
- The FOMC statement and dot plot on Wednesday will be interpreted through the lens of this weekend's developments. If the SEP shows materially higher inflation forecasts with lower GDP, expect a bear-flattening move (long end stays, short end rises on hawkish pricing).
- Equity futures pointed lower Friday evening, with Dow futures down ~300 points in after-hours on the Trump no-ceasefire comments.

If the dot plot shifts hawkish (zero cuts in 2026), discount rates for PPA valuations rise, compressing NPVs. However, the Amazon $37B bond deal signals hyperscalers are locking in current rates aggressively — they see this window closing.

- **Critical Minerals:** Capital-intensive mining projects are the most rate-sensitive thesis leg. The combination of higher rates + geopolitical supply disruption (Iran conflict) creates a mixed signal: higher financing costs but stronger strategic urgency for domestic supply chains.
