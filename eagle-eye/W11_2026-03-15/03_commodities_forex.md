## Energy

### Crude Oil

The Iran war remains the dominant force in energy markets. Brent crude closed Friday at **$103.14/bbl**, up ~10% on the week, after surging intraday near $120/bbl earlier in the week. WTI settled at **$98.71/bbl**, up ~8% week-over-week. This follows the prior week's 27.9% surge — the biggest weekly gain since 2020. Both benchmarks traded above $100 for the second consecutive session on Friday.

**Key drivers:**
- **Strait of Hormuz effectively shut down.** Oil tanker traffic through the strait — which handles ~20 million bbl/day or 25% of global seaborne oil trade — has been at less than 10% of pre-conflict levels since the U.S.-Israel strikes on Iran began February 28. On March 12, only one vessel was observed transiting the waterway.
- **IEA record reserve release.** On March 11, IEA's 32 member countries unanimously agreed to release **400 million barrels** from strategic reserves — the largest coordinated release ever and only the sixth time the IEA has taken such action. The U.S. will contribute 172 million barrels from the SPR over ~120 days. Despite this, oil prices quickly rebounded and remained above $90/bbl within hours of the announcement.
- **OPEC+ production increase.** On March 1, OPEC+ agreed to begin unwinding voluntary cuts by adding **206,000 b/d** in April (Saudi Arabia and Russia each +62,000 b/d, Iraq +26,000, UAE +18,000, Kuwait +16,000, Kazakhstan +10,000, Algeria +6,000). Saudi Arabia had already ramped output to 10.882 million b/d in February, up from 10.1 million in January.
- **EIA inventory data (week ending March 6):** U.S. crude inventories rose 3.824 million barrels (vs. 1.1M forecast), the third consecutive weekly build. Total inventories at 443.1 million barrels, ~2% below five-year average. Refinery inputs averaged 16.2 million b/d (90.8% utilization).

### Natural Gas

Henry Hub spot traded at **~$3.25/MMBtu** (as of March 9), well below the 2026 forecast average of $3.80/MMBtu. Mild February weather and higher-than-expected storage levels have capped upside. Working gas in storage hit **1,848 Bcf** (week ending March 6) — 141 Bcf above year-ago levels and near the five-year average. EIA expects inventories to end the withdrawal season at ~1,840 Bcf.

U.S. marketed natural gas production set a new record in 2025, growing 5.3 Bcf/d to 118.5 Bcf/d. Despite the Middle East disruption to LNG flows through Hormuz, domestic supply abundance has kept Henry Hub prices subdued.

## Metals

### Gold

Spot gold closed Friday at approximately **$5,020-$5,119/oz** (sources vary by reporting time), consolidating around the psychologically important $5,000 level. Gold posted a modest weekly decline of approximately **-1% to -3%**, pulled lower by a strengthening dollar despite robust safe-haven demand.

**Crosscurrents:** The typical geopolitical safe-haven bid has been partially offset by the surging dollar, which gained ~1% over the past five trading sessions. Rising oil prices have stoked fresh inflation concerns, pushing traders to reduce bets on Fed rate cuts — a headwind for non-yielding gold. The OANDA weekly forecast highlighted a "3% slide to $5,000/oz as rate-cut bets tumble." Despite the weekly pullback, gold remains up dramatically year-over-year, reflecting persistent sovereign-risk concerns and central bank buying.

### Silver

Silver spot closed at **$83.85-$84.44/oz** on Friday, down ~2.2% on the day and under pressure from dollar strength. Silver has gained more than $50/oz over the past year, reflecting the broader precious metals rally, but faces near-term headwinds from the same dollar dynamics weighing on gold.

### Copper

LME copper spot was approximately **$12,678/tonne** (~$5.75/lb) as of March 13. The market showed modest weekly gains, with scrap copper (a proxy indicator) up ~0.5% for the March 6-12 week. LME and SHFE copper eased slightly into Friday's close.

**Structural thesis intact.** AI data center and EV demand continue to underpin the long-term copper bull case. JPMorgan expects copper at $12,500/tonne by Q2 2026, averaging ~$12,075 for the full year. UBS targets $13,000 by year-end. The ICSG projects the refined copper market to shift to a **150,000-tonne deficit in 2026**, reversing previous surplus forecasts.

**Thesis tickers:** Freeport-McMoRan (FCX) trades with copper near historic highs of ~$6.00/lb. FCX reported $25.9B revenue and $9.9B adjusted EBITDA in 2025. Southern Copper (SCCO) also well-positioned.

## Commodities

### Uranium

Spot uranium retreated to approximately **$85.90/lb** in early March, down from January's two-year high of $94.28/lb. The February close was $86.95/lb.

**Headwinds:** A strengthening dollar and market focus shifting to near-term crude oil/energy security rather than nuclear infrastructure. Uzbekistan's Atomic Energy Agency boosted annual production output and raised long-term reserve estimates, temporarily cooling structural deficit fears.

**Thesis check:** Spot price at $85.90/lb sits at the low end of the thesis range ($85-100/lb). The 30-40M lb annual supply deficit thesis remains intact longer-term, though near-term sentiment is overshadowed by the Iran conflict. Cameco (CCJ) signed a nine-year supply agreement to deliver ~22 million lbs U3O8 to India's Department of Atomic Energy (deliveries 2027-2035). CCJ stock at ~$107-110; analyst consensus Buy with $123.92 target (+14.8%).

**Thesis tickers:** CCJ, UEC, UUUU, NXE, DNN.

### Lithium

Lithium carbonate spot is flat at approximately **$23.06/kg** (~$23,060/tonne) as of mid-March, with Chinese carbonate at ~CNY 159,000/tonne. Regional prices vary: $17.38/kg in Northeast Asia, $13.36/kg in Europe, $7.64/kg in South America (February data).

**Thesis check:** At ~$23,000/tonne, lithium has recovered modestly above the thesis target of ~$20,000/tonne. Mixed forces prevail: Zimbabwe's suspension of lithium concentrate exports and revocation of 27 mining permits in China's Jiangxi hub support prices, while battery makers restock ahead of VAT export rebate cuts. Softening global EV sales provide an offset. MIT Technology Review flagged 2026 as a "hot year" for lithium as supply-demand tightens.

**Thesis tickers:** ALB, SQM.

### Rare Earths

NdPr oxide reached **$103,758/tonne** ($103.76/kg) on China's domestic market as of March 10 — approximately double December 2024 levels. More critically, the FOB China export price was **$184/kg**, a **63% premium** over the domestic price, reflecting acute supply chain security premiums.

NdPr oxide gained **42% YTD** through late February, reaching $111.5/kg on Feb 25.

**China export controls update:** The October 2025 expansion of controls remains in force, requiring foreign companies to obtain licenses for "parts, components and assemblies" containing Chinese-sourced rare earth materials. As of December 2025, controls extended to "internationally made" products containing Chinese-sourced materials. Five additional elements (holmium, erbium, thulium, europium, ytterbium) were added to restrictions. China also launched a new Rare Earth Price Index to exert further pricing control.

**Thesis check:** The 63% domestic-vs-export price spread is the clearest signal of how China's export controls are creating a structural premium for non-Chinese supply. MP Materials (MP) produced its first commercial NdFeB magnets at its Fort Worth facility in December 2025 and began ramping supply to GM in early 2026. MP traded at ~$57-60, with Q4 2025 marking its first GAAP-profitable quarter (net income $9.4M).

**Thesis tickers:** MP (critical watch — first U.S. NdFeB magnet production now commercial).

### Agricultural Commodities

March SRW wheat at **~$5.98/bu**, May corn futures at **~$4.61/bu**. Grain markets rebounded mid-week on technical buying, biofuel demand optimism, and steady export activity. USDA projects 2026 marketing-year averages of $4.20/bu for corn, $10.30/bu for soybeans, and $5.00/bu for wheat — near or below breakeven cost of production. The Middle East conflict raises concerns about global food inflation through disruptions to energy, fertilizer, and shipping supply chains.

## Supply Chain

### Baltic Dry Index

The BDI stood at **2,066** on March 9 (up 56 points), with a range of 1,882-2,242 over the prior month. The index fell to a six-month low of 1,882 during early March on weaker iron ore cargo demand and seasonal effects before recovering.

### Container Shipping / Hormuz Crisis

The Strait of Hormuz closure has triggered a **dual chokepoint crisis** — Hormuz and the Red Sea (Houthi attacks resumed February 28). Major carriers (Maersk, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd) suspended Hormuz transits.

- **Spot rates spiked 750-900%** on select Gulf routes. China-UAE rates rose 5% in just ten days preceding the strikes, reaching $1,572/FEU.
- **Maersk Emergency Bunker Surcharge** effective March 25: $200/TEU and $400/FEU on all long-haul headhaul routes.
- **War risk premiums** surging from 0.25% to 0.5%+ of insured hull value. For a $150M vessel, that is $375,000-$750,000 per transit.
- UAE's Fujairah port operations temporarily suspended after drone strikes, then resumed.

The supply chain disruption is the most severe since the Suez Canal blockage, compounded by the simultaneous Red Sea closure forcing Cape of Good Hope rerouting — adding weeks to transit times and layering costs across global trade.

## Weekend Developments (March 14-15)

**THIS IS THE MOST CRITICAL SECTION. Major escalation over the weekend.**

### Kharg Island Strikes (March 13-15)

On Friday March 13, Trump announced U.S. forces had "totally obliterated" all military targets on Iran's **Kharg Island** — the oil export hub handling 90% of Iran's crude exports. CENTCOM confirmed destruction of 90+ military targets while stating oil infrastructure was "preserved." On Saturday, Trump told NBC News the U.S. "may hit it a few more times just for fun," and on Sunday the administration confirmed it was **actively considering strikes on Iran's oil infrastructure** itself.

### Iran Retaliation

Iran's IRGC carried out missile and drone strikes on targets in Israel and three U.S. bases in the region on Sunday, calling it "the first round of retaliation." Iran's Foreign Minister explicitly denied asking for a ceasefire: *"No, we never asked for a ceasefire, and we have never asked even for negotiation."* Tehran threatened to reduce U.S.-linked oil facilities to "a pile of ashes."

### Market Implications for Monday

The weekend escalation is **severely bearish for risk assets and bullish for oil, gold, and the dollar:**
- Trump's threat to strike Iran's oil infrastructure directly (not just military targets on Kharg) could take 3-4 million b/d of Iranian exports permanently offline.
- Iran's retaliatory strikes on U.S. bases and Israel signal no de-escalation path.
- A Fortune strategist predicts "peak war panic" will hit financial markets within 1-3 weeks.
- Oil futures (which open Sunday evening) likely to gap significantly higher. Analysts warn prices need to reach $150/bbl before demand destruction rebalances the market.

### SPR / Policy Response

The Trump administration held discussions about taking positions in crude oil futures markets to suppress prices (per Interior Secretary Burgum, March 14). The IEA's 400M-barrel reserve release is underway but market consensus is that it "cannot fix Hormuz" — it only addresses panic, not the structural loss of a critical shipping corridor.

## The Week Ahead

### FOMC Meeting (March 17-18) — The Main Event

The Fed's two-day meeting concludes Wednesday March 18 at 2:00 PM ET with a policy statement and updated Summary of Economic Projections (dot plot). Chair Powell's press conference follows at 2:30 PM.

- **Rate decision:** CME FedWatch shows 92%+ probability the Fed holds at **3.50%-3.75%**. A hold is essentially priced in.
- **What matters:** The dot plot and SEP will reveal how officials interpret the Iran war's impact on inflation. With PCE at 2.8%, GDP revised down to 0.7%, and oil at $100+, the Fed faces a classic stagflation dilemma. Markets have abandoned early-summer rate-cut hopes; only one cut is priced for all of 2026, possibly September.
- **Dollar/commodity impact:** A hawkish hold (acknowledging inflation risks from energy) would further strengthen the dollar and pressure gold. Any dovish surprise acknowledging growth risks could weaken the dollar and lift gold toward $5,200+.

### Other Key Events

- **OPEC+ next meeting:** April 5 — will reassess the 206,000 b/d April production increase amid the unfolding crisis.
- **Iran war trajectory:** Day 16+ of strikes. No ceasefire path visible. Escalation risk is rising, not falling.
- **EIA weekly petroleum status report** (Wednesday) — will be closely watched for real-time inventory impacts of the Hormuz closure.
- **ECB speakers** throughout the week as markets digest the dramatic swing to rate-hike expectations.
