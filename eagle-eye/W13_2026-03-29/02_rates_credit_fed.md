## Treasury Yields

| Maturity | Yield (3/27) | Weekly Change (bps) | 4-Week Change (vs 2/27) |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 2-Year   | 3.93%       | +5 bps             | +55 bps               |
| 5-Year   | 4.07%       | ~+5 bps            | ~+50 bps              |
| 10-Year  | 4.44%       | +5 bps             | +47 bps               |
| 30-Year  | 4.97-4.98%  | +2 bps             | ~+40 bps              |

**Narrative:** Treasury yields surged to their highest levels since mid-2025, capping a month of relentless selling driven by the Iran war's inflationary shock. The 10-year hit 4.44% on March 27 -- its highest since July 2025 -- after starting the month near 4.00%. The 30-year briefly touched 5.00% intraday on Friday, a psychologically significant level. The weekly move was modest (+2-5 bps across the curve) compared to the explosive March-to-date repricing: the 2-year spiked 55 bps in four weeks as rate-cut expectations evaporated, while the 10-year added 47 bps as inflation fears from oil prices overwhelmed safe-haven demand.

Brent crude at ~$112/bbl (up 58% from pre-conflict levels) is the primary driver. The Strait of Hormuz closure disrupted 20% of global oil supply, and despite a record 400M-barrel SPR release, prices remain elevated. Treasury bonds have "defied safe haven status" -- investors are demanding higher yields as oil-driven inflation expectations dominate growth fears.

A weak $69B 2-year note auction on March 24 amplified the selloff, with poor demand reflecting investor reluctance to buy short-dated paper when the Fed path is this uncertain. The MOVE index (bond volatility) surged above 130 in late March, signaling extreme stress -- well above the "calm" range below 60.

## Yield Curve

| Spread         | Level (3/27) | Direction           |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| 2s10s          | +51 bps     | Steepening          |
| 3m10y          | +71 bps     | Positive, widening  |

**Narrative:** The yield curve underwent a dramatic regime change in March. As Wolf Street documented on March 21, the curve "solidly uninverted" as yields in the 1-year to 5-year belly rose the most, eliminating the sag that had persisted for three years when middle-maturity yields were lower than both short- and long-term yields.

The 2s10s spread widened to roughly +51 bps (from ~+46 bps as of March 19), a bear steepening driven by long-end yields rising on term premium and inflation fears. The 3m10y spread stands at +71 bps, firmly positive.

Critically, this is **not** a bullish steepening (where the front end falls on expected cuts). This is a bear steepener: the long end is rising faster than the short end because markets are pricing in (a) persistent inflation from oil, (b) wider deficits from defense spending, and (c) reduced confidence in Fed easing. The 2-year yield spiked 53 bps in March, flipping from pricing in rate cuts to pricing in the potential for a rate hike -- a dramatic shift.

## Credit Markets

| Index | OAS (bps) | Direction |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| IG (ICE BofA Corporate) | ~120 bps | Widening from ~83-100 bps range |
| HY (ICE BofA HY) | 317-321 bps | Modest widening |
| BB | 199 bps | Relatively tight |
| CCC & Lower | 974 bps | Significant stress |

**Narrative:** Credit spreads have widened from their multi-decade tights of early 2025, but the move has been orderly rather than dislocating. IG spreads at ~120 bps are above the 83-112 bps range that held through most of 2024 but still below long-term averages (~130-150 bps). HY spreads at ~320 bps are wider than the sub-300 tights but not yet signaling broad distress.

The bifurcation by credit quality is notable: BB-rated credits remain tight at 199 bps, while CCC & Lower have blown out to 974 bps. This reflects stress concentrated in the most leveraged, energy-sensitive, and speculative segments -- not a broad risk-off event. Distressed exchanges accounted for 56% of all defaults through 2025, and Moody's expects the US HY probability of default to rise to 7.9% in Q1 2026.

**Issuance:** Through February 2026, YTD corporate bond issuance was $484.9B (+12.4% YoY). The IG primary market remained active, with 2026 forecasts as high as $2.25T in investment-grade issuance (+35% YoY). However, rising yields and widening spreads are expected to slow the pace, particularly for lower-rated issuers.

**Key risk:** If oil prices remain elevated and the Fed shifts to a hiking bias, the CCC segment could face a real credit crunch. The 974 bps spread on CCC & Lower is approaching stress territory.

## Liquidity

### Fed Balance Sheet & Reserve Management
- **Total Fed assets:** $6.7T (as of March 25, 2026)
- **Reserve balances:** $3.02T (up $83B since December)
- **QT status:** Ended December 1, 2025, after reducing the balance sheet from $8.9T peak to $6.5T. The Fed reversed course to "Reserve Management Purchases" (RMPs) -- buying ~$40B/month in T-bills since early December to maintain "ample reserves." Total RMP additions: $93B through late March. The Fed plans to "significantly reduce" RMPs after Tax Day (mid-April).
- **MBS rolloff:** Continuing passively. MBS proceeds are being reinvested into Treasuries, gradually converting the Fed's mortgage portfolio into an all-Treasury balance sheet.

### Treasury General Account (TGA)
- **Balance:** ~$871B (as of March 20, 2026), up from ~$806B on March 13.
- The TGA is being rebuilt after the debt ceiling resolution, which acts as a liquidity drain from the banking system (cash moves from bank reserves into the TGA).

### Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP)
- **Balance:** Near zero (~$10-20B range).
- The ON RRP facility, which peaked above $2T in 2022-2023, has been effectively drained as T-bill yields exceed the ON RRP rate (3.50%), making money market funds prefer bills over parking at the Fed.
- **Implication:** With the ON RRP buffer gone, every marginal liquidity shock falls directly on bank reserves. The Fed's RMPs are the replacement buffer mechanism.

### Liquidity Assessment
Liquidity conditions are adequate but fragile. The $3.02T in reserves is considered "ample" by the Fed, and the $40B/month in RMPs provides ongoing support. However, the near-zero ON RRP means the system lacks the excess liquidity cushion it had in 2022-2023. The TGA rebuild (~$871B) is absorbing cash from the system. When the Fed tapers RMPs after mid-April, reserve levels will need monitoring. The March MOVE index spike above 130 reflects this underlying fragility -- bond market plumbing is strained even if not broken.

## Weekend Developments (Post-Friday 3/27 Close)

### Strait of Hormuz Remains Shut
As of Saturday March 28, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. Brent crude is at ~$112/bbl, WTI near $100. CNBC reports that oil executives warn the strait must reopen by mid-April or disruptions will "get significantly worse." The record 400M-barrel SPR release (the largest ever) and temporary sanctions relief on Russian/Iranian oil are providing only partial relief. Goldman Sachs projects Q2 2026 Brent averaging $110, with an extreme upside scenario of $135 if disruption continues 6+ months.

### Fed Chair Powell Speaks Monday (March 30)
Per the next-week economic calendar, Powell is scheduled to speak at 2:30 PM ET on Monday March 30. This is the first public appearance since the FOMC presser on March 18. Markets will parse every word for any shift in the "conditional on the data" framework, particularly regarding whether oil-driven inflation is viewed as transitory or risks becoming entrenched. Williams also speaks Monday evening.

### Key Risk for Week Ahead
The week of March 30 - April 3 is packed: Powell (Monday), JOLTS + Consumer Confidence (Tuesday), ISM Manufacturing (Wednesday), and NFP Jobs Report (Friday April 3). Any combination of hot inflation data + weak employment could crystallize stagflation fears and push the 10-year toward 4.50%+ and the 30-year above 5%.

### No Emergency Fed Actions
No emergency meetings, policy changes, or extraordinary interventions announced as of Saturday evening.
