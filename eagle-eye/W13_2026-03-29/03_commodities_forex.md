## Energy

### Crude Oil

The Strait of Hormuz crisis dominated energy markets for the fourth consecutive week. Iran's rejection of US peace talks on Thursday and continued near-total closure of the strait (~90-95% traffic reduction) pushed crude to multi-year highs by Friday's close.

| Benchmark | Friday Close | Weekly Change | Intraday Range (Fri) |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|----------------------|
| WTI (CL) | **$99.64/bbl** | +~$10/bbl (~11%) | $92.08 – $101.24 |
| Brent (BZ) | **$112.57/bbl** | +~$7/bbl (+4.2% Fri alone) | — |

WTI briefly crossed $100 intraday (touching $100.04), a level not seen since 2022. Brent settled at its highest since July 2022. Goldman Sachs estimates a $14-18/bbl geopolitical risk premium baked into current prices. Dubai physical crude (reflecting actual Middle East delivery) is even more extreme, up 76% since the conflict began, trading at ~$126/bbl — more than double the paper-price gain.

**Weekly narrative:** Prices pulled back early in the week to the $89-97 range (WTI) on ceasefire rumors, then surged sharply after Iran's Foreign Minister declared no negotiations with the US were planned. President Trump extended his deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait by 10 days (to ~April 6), but this failed to calm markets. On Thursday, Trump claimed Iran let 10 oil ships through as a "present" — but analysts noted this was negligible against the 17.8 mb/d of flows lost.

**OPEC+:** In early March, OPEC+ agreed to resume production increases at a modest 206,000 b/d for April (debating options from 137K-548K b/d before settling). The decision was described as "more about signaling than volume" — OPEC+ showing willingness to deploy spare capacity without flooding the market during the supply crisis.

This was the **fifth consecutive weekly crude build**, with Cushing hub inventories surging +3.421 mb (largest weekly rise since January 2023). Despite the Hormuz disruption choking Middle East flows, US crude is building as refiners process domestic and non-Gulf supply. Refinery utilization was 92.9% (16.6 mb/d inputs). The crude builds are paradoxically bearish near-term but bullish structurally — they reflect rerouting, not demand destruction.

### Natural Gas

Henry Hub spot closed at **$3.05/MMBtu**, roughly flat on the week. Natural gas has traded in a narrow $3.00-$3.20 range through March despite the Hormuz crisis, as increased LNG supply diversion to Europe was offset by mild US weather reducing domestic heating demand. Asian LNG prices (JKM) are up ~48% since the conflict began, creating a widening transatlantic spread. The EIA forecasts Henry Hub averaging ~$3.80/MMBtu for full-year 2026.

Gold endured another volatile week, caught between competing forces: safe-haven demand from the Iran war vs. a strengthening dollar and surging real yields. The hawkish Fed pivot — dot plot now showing zero rate cuts for 2026, vs. three expected earlier in the year — has been the dominant headwind since Fed Chair nominee Kevin Warsh's January 30 confirmation. Real Treasury yields have risen to ~4.2%, crushing the non-yielding metal.

Mid-week, gold plunged below $4,400, hitting a four-month low before bouncing 3.6% to ~$4,536 on safe-haven buying after Iran rejected talks. By Friday close, it had settled back to ~$4,431. The week represented the tail end of what analysts described as the worst stretch of gold price declines since 2013.

### Critical Minerals (Thesis Relevant)

**Lithium:**
Battery-grade lithium carbonate has pushed above **~$20,000/tonne**, with spodumene at $2,000/tonne. Northeast Asia lithium metal prices declined 9.4% in March to $16.88/kg, reflecting temporary supply adjustments, but the broader trend is firmly upward from the $10,000 lows of 2024. The surplus is narrowing toward near-balance in 2026, with energy storage demand emerging as the fastest-growing consumption driver. Key tickers: ALB, SQM, PLS.AX, LAC.

**Rare Earths / NdPr:**
NdPr oxide surged to **~$108.64/kg** (SMM spot), representing a **~105% gain** since January (~$53/kg). This dramatic rally tracks stronger-than-expected Q4 2025/Q1 2026 EV registration data from China and Europe. Neodymium oxide at $117.27/kg. China's export control architecture remains intact despite the November 2025 temporary suspension (expires November 2026). Key tickers: MP, LYC.AX/LYSCF, UUUU, USAR.

**Cobalt:**
Cobalt traded at **~$56,290/tonne**, up ~2.65% on the month and +161% YoY. DRC export quotas (96,000 MT/year, roughly half 2024 volumes) continue to support elevated pricing. European cobalt metal prices are forecast to rise further in Q2 2026 as DRC shipment delays intensify.

## Commodities

### Agricultural

Grains rallied modestly alongside oil and added weather premium during the week.

| Commodity | Friday Close (May '26) | Weekly Direction |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Corn (CBOT) | **462.00 c/bu** | Up 2-3 c |
| Wheat (CBOT) | **605.00 c/bu** | Up 4-5 c |
| Soybeans | Modestly higher (+3-4 c) | Up |

The Iran war has not yet materially disrupted grain flows, but fertilizer markets are at risk — roughly one-third of global fertilizer trade transits the Strait of Hormuz, including large volumes of nitrogen exports from the Gulf states. If the closure persists into planting season, this could become a significant price driver.

US corn planted acreage is forecast at 94.0M acres (down 4.8M YoY), with March 1 stockpiles expected at ~9.04B bushels (+11% YoY). Brazil's record soybean crop (184.7 MMT) is pressuring soy prices despite the broader commodity bid.

**DXY Narrative:** The dollar index held just above 100, posting a ~0.3% weekly gain after three consecutive daily advances. The greenback's strength reflects two converging forces: (1) safe-haven demand from the Hormuz crisis, and (2) the hawkish Fed repricing — markets now price a ~50% chance of a rate *hike* by December, a dramatic reversal from earlier expectations of two cuts. The dollar strengthened most against GBP and CHF, while holding roughly flat against EUR and JPY.

**USD/JPY:** The yen weakened to 160.29, approaching intervention-watch levels. Japan's massive oil import dependency makes it uniquely vulnerable to the Hormuz disruption. The BOJ faces a difficult balancing act — higher energy costs warrant tighter policy, but a slowing global economy argues against aggressive rate hikes.

**EM Currencies:** Turkish lira continues controlled depreciation (consensus USD/TRY 42-46 range for 2026), offering the best carry trade in EM per ING. Brazilian real remains under pressure from fiscal concerns, with the government actively intervening via dollar sales to stabilize the currency. Oil-importing EM currencies (INR, TRY, ZAR) face the worst of both worlds — higher import bills and dollar strength.

## Weekend Developments (Post-Friday Close, March 28-29)

### Iran / Hormuz — Critical Situation Deepening

The biggest oil supply shock in history entered its one-month mark this weekend. Key developments:

- **Trump extended the Hormuz reopening deadline by 10 days** (to ~April 6), but continued to claim talks were "going well." Markets are skeptical — WTI closed at $99.64, and oil executives warn the Strait must reopen by mid-April or disruptions double.
- **Iran allowed limited transits** for ships owned by China, Russia, India, Iraq, Pakistan, Malaysia, and Thailand — but Western-flagged vessels remain blocked. This selective reopening is geopolitically significant but oil-volume-irrelevant.
- **Oil analysts modeling $200/bbl scenarios** if closure persists through April. The world has lost ~4.5-5 mb/d (5% of global supply); this doubles by mid-April as storage fills and Gulf producers are forced to cut further.
- **Refined fuel crisis emerging:** Diesel and jet fuel prices in Asia have topped $200/bbl at times, with the first signs of demand destruction in Asian markets. Europe faces diesel shortage risk in coming weeks.
- **LNG prices (JKM)** up 48% since conflict began. Helium and fertilizer markets also severely disrupted.

### Gold Weekend Pricing

OTC/indicative gold pricing over the weekend shows continued recovery: ~$4,493 (Saturday) to ~$4,514 (Sunday). This suggests the Friday lows near $4,431 may represent near-term support, with safe-haven flows returning as the Hormuz crisis deepens.

### Silver Weekend Pricing

Weekend OTC silver indicated at ~$72-74, a notable recovery from Friday's $68.20 close. Markets appear to be repricing the excessive correction.

### No Material OPEC Weekend Announcements

OPEC+ did not make additional production announcements over the weekend. The April 206,000 b/d increase stands as planned.
