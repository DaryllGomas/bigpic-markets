# Eagle Eye — Commodities & Forex (Agent 3)
**Point-in-time snapshot: Sunday, June 7, 2026** — covering the trading week ending Friday June 5, 2026, plus weekend/breaking developments through Sunday June 7.

> **Macro frame for this week (essential context):** Commodities and FX this week cannot be read normally. Since **Feb 28, 2026**, the US + Israel have been at war with Iran ("Operation Epic Fury"); Iran declared the **Strait of Hormuz "closed" on March 4, 2026** and it remains effectively shut. The IEA calls this the **largest oil supply disruption in the history of the global oil market** (~20% of world oil trade + ~20% of traded LNG routed through Hormuz). The dominant cross-asset story is an **energy-driven inflation shock that has flipped the Fed toward HIKING** — markets now price a quarter-point Fed rate **increase** by year-end. That flipped-rate expectation, reinforced by a hot May jobs report (172k vs 85k expected, released Fri Jun 5), is why gold, silver and copper SOLD OFF hard this week even with a hot war ongoing, while the dollar and crude rose. Treat all "war context" as ongoing/structural; treat the weekly moves as the new marginal news.

## Energy

**Crude oil — up on the week on renewed US–Iran clashes; pulled back Friday.**
- **WTI:** closed **~$91/bbl Friday Jun 5, 2026, down ~3.1% on the day** but **up ~4–6% on the week** (Trading Economics). The weekly gain came after renewed clashes between US and Iranian forces dampened hopes for a deal to reopen Hormuz. President Trump said the strait "could reopen quickly" if Iran agrees to an MOU to halt hostilities, but little concrete progress emerged; Israel's continued operations in Lebanon remain an obstacle.
- **Brent:** **~$94/bbl Friday Jun 5**, **up ~3% on the week** (Trading Economics). A separate Fortune snapshot showed Brent at **$97.44 at 9:00am ET Jun 5** (a daily/spot read, not the Friday settle) — note the discrepancy; the ~$94 settle is the more reliable weekly close.
- **War premium framing:** crude is elevated vs. pre-war levels but well off panic highs; Fortune notes Brent is **+48% year-over-year** but **−17.9% month-over-month**, i.e., prices have been grinding lower from the March/April spike even as the strait stays shut, on demand-destruction fears from a looming Fed hike and global-growth worry.

**OPEC+ — BREAKING Sunday Jun 7: fourth quota hike since Hormuz closure.**
- On **Sunday June 7, 2026**, OPEC+ raised **July output targets by 188,000 bpd** — its fourth straight monthly increase since April. Cumulative quota increases from the seven core members (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, Algeria, Kazakhstan, Russia, Oman) total **~600,000 bpd from April to June**.
- Allocation (per Trading Economics/CNBC reporting): Saudi Arabia and Russia each +62,000 bpd (to 10.291M and 9.762M bpd respectively); Iraq +26,000; Kuwait +16,000; Kazakhstan +10,000; Algeria +6,000; Oman +5,000.
- **Key irony / supply reality:** the quota hikes are largely symbolic — the war has cut flows via Hormuz so badly that key members **including Saudi Arabia have been unable to supply customers in full since end-February**. Paper quota up, physical barrels still stranded. Monthly increment was trimmed from ~206k bpd (Apr/May) after the UAE's exit from the group.

**Natural gas / LNG — domestic Henry Hub firm; international LNG in crisis.**
- **US Henry Hub:** futures pushed **past $3.30/MMBtu in early June (4-month high)** but finished the week **down >1%**. Drivers: above-normal temps forecast through Jun 20 (cooling demand) vs. softer LNG feedgas (export flows fell to ~16.4 Bcf/d in June from 17.1 in May on seasonal maintenance at Golden Pass and Freeport). EIA reported a **+95 Bcf storage injection for the week ended May 29**, below both the year-ago and 5-yr-average builds.
- **International LNG (Hormuz-driven):** ~10+ Bcf/d of global LNG (~20% of traded LNG, mostly Qatar's Ras Laffan) sidelined. Per EIA, no laden LNG tankers crossed Hormuz Mar 1–Apr 24. By the week of Apr 24, **TTF (Europe) ~$14.80/MMBtu (+35% vs pre-closure)** and **JKM (Asia) ~$16.02/MMBtu (+51%)**. Europe entered summer refill with storage only ~28% full (vs 41% 5-yr avg) and has pulled record Russian Yamal LNG (~£3bn Jan–Apr) to compensate. *(These LNG levels are late-April figures — flagged as the latest verified international LNG prints, not a Friday Jun 5 close.)*

**EIA inventories:** Latest Weekly Petroleum Status Report covers **week ended May 29 (released Jun 3)**: **US crude stocks 441.7M bbl, ~2% below the 5-yr average**. The week-ended-Jun-4 report is **not yet released (due Jun 10)** — a data gap for this snapshot.

## Metals

**Precious metals sold off hard — the week's most counterintuitive move.** A hot-war backdrop normally lifts gold; instead the energy-inflation-driven shift to Fed *hiking* expectations + a strong dollar crushed metals.

- **Gold:** dropped **below $4,370/oz Friday Jun 5**, the **lowest since March 2026**, for a **weekly decline of ~4%** — its sharpest weekly retreat in a month. A separate intraday read had gold at **$4,408 at 9:30am ET Jun 5** before sliding. Drivers: the May NFP beat (172k vs 85k) pushed markets to price a **quarter-point Fed hike by year-end**; Hezbollah's rejection of a US-mediated Israel–Lebanon ceasefire added cross-currents but did not arrest the slide.
- **Silver:** fell **below $70/oz Friday Jun 5**, lowest since late March, **down >7% on the week** (worst weekly performer in the complex). Intraday prints: ~$73.03 (7:03am) → ~$71.21 (9:36am) ET, sliding post-jobs report.
- **Copper (demand signal — bearish tilt this week):** COMEX copper fell **~2% to ~$6.38/lb Friday Jun 5** (Trading Economics), retreating from a June record high of ~$6.67/lb. Earlier in the week it was firmer (COMEX ~$6.51/lb and LME 3-month ~$13,932/t as of Jun 4). The Friday drop reflects fear that a Fed hike + prolonged Hormuz disruption dents global growth and copper demand — a notable bearish demand read despite the structural copper-deficit thesis (FCX/SCCO). Net: copper roughly flat-to-up on the week but rolling over Friday.

**Uranium / nuclear fuel (watchlist: CCJ, UUUU, NXE, UEC):**
- **Spot U3O8 eased to below ~$85/lb in late May**, the lowest in ~2 months, down from a Jan 29, 2026 YTD high of $101.41/lb. Uranium has de-rated from early-year peaks despite the structural supply-deficit thesis.
- **Cameco (CCJ):** referenced trading around **~$103** on its investor page (note: this looks like a stale/illustrative figure on Cameco's site — treat the exact CCJ equity level as **unverified for the Jun 5 close**; equities are Agent-2 territory anyway). The relevant commodity takeaway: spot uranium softening into the low-to-mid $80s.

## Commodities (Agriculture & Freight)

**Agriculture — Iran-war fertilizer/energy shock is the through-line.**
- Hormuz closure halted Persian Gulf **nitrogen fertilizer exports**; nitrogen fertilizer prices up **30–40%**. Higher energy/fertilizer costs + biofuel demand pull have lifted soft commodities: **palm oil surged as much as 10%, soybean oil jumped, and wheat neared a ~2-year peak**.
- **US acreage response:** USDA survey points to the **smallest US wheat crop since 1919** and corn planting **down >3M acres** as farmers cut back on fertilizer-cost pressure. Row-crop farmers face a fourth straight year of losses; prices up modestly but barely above breakeven. *(Most of this farmdoc/USDA reporting is March–April 2026 — structural, not a fresh-this-week catalyst; flagged as context.)*

**Freight & shipping (Hormuz war-risk):**
- **War-risk insurance** for Hormuz transits surged to ~3–8% of hull value → **$3–8M per large-tanker transit** (vs. ~$40k normal). One outlet framed it as "4,000-times higher" insurance costs.
- **VLCC Middle East→China rates +154%** in the week after intervention; container Shanghai→Jebel Ali ~+55% MoM to ~$4,200/FEU. **Hormuz traffic down ~95%** vs. pre-war; ~100 container ships reportedly trapped in the Arabian Gulf. This is a persistent cost overhang feeding the inflation shock.

No reliable post-Friday-close commodity settles are available (markets closed over the weekend); the next real catalyst is how Monday opens against the Sunday OPEC+ decision and Day-100 escalation.
