## Bottom Line Up Front

The dominant macro driver of rates and credit this week was **geopolitics, not the Fed**: the **2026 Iran war / Strait of Hormuz crisis** and the on-again, off-again **US–Iran ceasefire negotiations**. Optimism over a 60-day ceasefire memorandum drove **oil down ~20% from its 2026 peak**, which in turn pulled Treasury yields lower across most maturities into Friday's close and supported a modest risk-on tone in credit. The 10-year fell from roughly **4.56%** at the prior week's close to around **4.44%** by Friday afternoon (June 5, 2026), per T. Rowe Price's weekly recap.

The structural backdrop has shifted hard from the start-of-year script. The Fed is now run by **new Chair Kevin Warsh** (confirmed by the Senate May 13, 2026; Powell's chair term ended May 15). War-driven energy inflation pushed **April CPI to 3.8% YoY** — the hottest since 2023 — and the market has flipped from pricing rate **cuts** to pricing an **extended pause with a meaningful tail risk of a HIKE**: CME-derived odds cited a **~60% probability of a rate hike at some point in 2026** and **<10% chance of any cut this year**. The next FOMC (**June 16–17, 2026**, Warsh's first as chair, with a fresh SEP/dot plot) is a near-certain hold.

Warsh chairs his **first FOMC on June 16–17, 2026**, which includes updated economic projections and the dot plot. [Schwab; Al Jazeera Apr 29, 2026]

**Policy stance: hawkish, extended pause, hike risk on the table.**
- Fed officials' inflation concerns "stoked by the Iran war intensified," with a growing number saying the central bank should "lay the groundwork for a possible rate hike" — Warsh inherits "an increasingly hawkish crew." A majority at the **April 28–29, 2026** meeting said some tightening "may be needed were inflation to continue running persistently above the 2% target." [NBC News]

## Credit Markets

**Direction this week: resilient to modestly tighter; risk-on tone from de-escalation.**
- High-yield bonds **generated positive returns** during the week, though "some gains faded as investors questioned the durability of geopolitical headlines and weighed hawkish Fed signaling." [T. Rowe Price]
- Recent weekly commentary noted credit spreads "proved resilient, with high yield tightening ~1 bp despite significant rate backup," and spreads "tightened modestly despite negative total return, reflecting continued demand for income and well-contained default expectations." [Nuveen/credit commentary] Strong technical demand (coupon/maturity reinvestment, oversubscribed deals) continues to anchor spreads tight.

**Level context (early/mid-2026, near-window but not June-5-specific):**
- **IG corporate OAS:** ~**80 bp** (BBB ~100 bp, AA ~50 bp) — historically tight. [investmentgrade.com]
- **HY OAS:** ~**285 bp** (one index reading cited 267 bp) — near 2007-era tights; "historically tight valuations." [investmentgrade.com; Janus Henderson; ETF Trends]

**Takeaway:** Despite the rate volatility and a war backdrop, **credit has NOT signaled stress** — IG and HY spreads sit near multi-year tights, supported by yield-buyer demand and contained default expectations. The risk is asymmetric: with spreads this compressed, there is little cushion if the ceasefire unravels or the Fed actually hikes.

> **Data flag:** Exact IG/HY OAS values dated June 5, 2026 were not confirmed; the levels above are early/mid-2026 readings. The **weekly direction** (resilient/modestly tighter, positive HY total return) is sourced to the week's commentary.
