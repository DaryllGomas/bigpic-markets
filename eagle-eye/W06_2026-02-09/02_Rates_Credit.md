## Summary

Treasuries rallied sharply mid-week on flight-to-safety flows triggered by weak labor data (ADP +22K vs +45K expected) and a tech/crypto selloff, before partially retracing Friday on a UMich sentiment beat. The 10Y fell as low as 3.74% on Wednesday before settling near 3.76% Thursday. The January NFP report was delayed to Feb 11 due to a brief government shutdown (Jan 31-Feb 4). The Fed remains on hold at 3.50-3.75%; markets now price ~58 bps of cuts by year-end (up from ~50 bps last week), with June the base case for the first move. Credit spreads remain tight but ticked wider on the week. The January SLOOS showed C&I lending standards tightened in Q4 while banks expect demand to strengthen in 2026.

## Fed Speakers

### Vice Chair Philip Jefferson (Feb 6 -- Brookings Institution)
Key speech of the week. Highlights:
- **Inflation:** PCE at 2.9%, core at 3.0% as of Dec 2025 -- unchanged from year-end 2024. Described disinflation as "stalled" but attributed it to tariff pass-through. Expects disinflation to resume once tariffs fully flow through.
- **Growth:** Q3 2025 GDP at 4.4%. Revised his 2026 growth forecast modestly higher, expecting growth similar to 2025's 2.2% pace.
- **Labor Market:** Unemployment at 4.4% in Dec 2025. Characterized the market as a "low-hiring, low-firing environment."
- **Policy:** Supported the January hold. Noted rates are "broadly in the range of estimates of the neutral rate." Policy stance should stabilize the labor market while letting inflation resume decline toward 2%.
- **Productivity:** Highlighted structural productivity growth at 2.2% annually since 2020 (vs. 1.5% pre-pandemic), partly driven by AI.

**Tone:** Balanced to slightly dovish. Comfortable on hold but acknowledged downside risks to employment.

### Gov. Bowman (Jan 30 -- just prior to this week)
- Discussed economic outlook and monetary policy. Hawkish lean -- voted for hold but signaled patience on further cuts given sticky inflation.

## Consumer Credit & Bank Lending

### ADP Employment (Released Feb 4)
- Private payrolls: +22,000 (vs. +45,000 expected, prior revised to +37,000)
- Only positive thanks to +74K in education/health services
- Professional/business services shed -57,000 jobs; manufacturing -8,000
- Pay growth: +4.5% YoY

### January NFP -- DELAYED
- The BLS Employment Situation report, originally scheduled for Feb 6, was delayed to **Feb 11** due to the partial government shutdown (Jan 31-Feb 4).
- Consensus expectation: +60,000 jobs

### Consumer Delinquencies (Q3 2025 -- latest available)
- Auto loan delinquency rate: 3.88% (up from 3.83% Q2), highest since post-GFC
- Credit card delinquencies: Transitions into serious delinquency increased
- Both flattening on a net basis heading into Q4 data

### January 2026 Senior Loan Officer Survey (SLOOS)
Released this week (60 domestic banks, 18 foreign branches):

| Category               | Standards       | Demand            |
|------------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| C&I (all sizes)        | Tightened       | Stronger (large)  |
| CRE                    | Unchanged       | Stronger          |
| Residential RE         | Unchanged       | Weaker            |
| Auto Loans             | Eased           | Weaker            |
| Credit Cards           | Unchanged       | Unchanged         |

- Banks cited uncertain economic outlook and reduced risk tolerance for C&I tightening
- Forward outlook: Banks expect standards to remain unchanged and demand to strengthen across all categories in 2026
- Notable: Banks reported being more likely to approve loans to firms with high AI exposure and less likely for those adversely affected by AI

## Forward Outlook

### What's Priced In for 2026
- **Rate path:** ~58 bps of cuts (2x 25 bps), up from ~50 bps entering the week
- **First cut timing:** June remains the base case; March nearly ruled out (~15%)
- **Second cut:** September or December, with September gaining probability
- **Terminal rate:** Market sees the Fed settling around 3.00-3.25% by late 2026/early 2027
- **No 2027 cuts priced in** -- markets expect the easing cycle to end in 2026

### Key Risks & Catalysts Ahead
1. **Jan NFP (Feb 11):** The delayed jobs report is the single most important data point. A weak print (<40K) could accelerate cut expectations toward May. A surprise upside (>80K) would push June pricing back.
2. **Jan CPI (also delayed by shutdown):** Inflation data will be critical given Jefferson's "stalled disinflation" framing.
3. **FOMC Minutes (Feb 18):** Will reveal how close the Committee was to cutting in January (Miran/Waller dissented in favor of a cut).
4. **Treasury Refunding:** $125B in quarterly refunding announced (sizes unchanged). Auctions next week: $58B 3Y, $42B 10Y, $25B 30Y. Strong demand expected given the rally.
5. **Tariff uncertainty:** Jefferson flagged tariff pass-through as the key inflation wildcard. Any escalation could push cut expectations back.

### Positioning Implications
- The mid-week rally created an opportunity: 10Y at 3.74% was the richest level in weeks
- Curve steepening bias remains intact (2s10s trending wider)
- Credit spreads at tight levels offer limited cushion -- favor quality over reach
- Mortgage market positioning for spring: if 10Y sustains below 3.80%, mortgage rates could drift toward 6.00%, further boosting purchase activity
