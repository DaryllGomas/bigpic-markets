## Treasury Yields

A violent week for rates. The convergence of the March FOMC meeting (March 18), Triple Witching/S&P rebalance (March 20), $606B in Treasury issuance, and the ongoing Iran war/Strait of Hormuz closure drove yields sharply higher across the curve. This was the biggest weekly move since July 2025.

| Maturity | Close 3/20 | Close 3/13 | Weekly Change | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **2-Year** | 3.88% | 3.73% | +15 bps | Highest since July 2025; pricing out rate cuts entirely |
| **3-Year** | 3.92% | ~3.75% | +17 bps | Highest since before last three 2025 rate cuts |
| **5-Year** | 3.88% | ~3.72% | +16 bps | Middle of the curve rose the most per Wolf Street |
| **10-Year** | 4.39% | 4.28% | +11 bps | Highest since July 2025 |
| **30-Year** | 4.96% | 4.90% | +6 bps | Flirting with 5% handle; mortgage rates hit 6.5% |

**Key dynamic:** The short end (1Y-5Y) rose the most, reflecting hawkish repricing of Fed expectations. The 2Y yield has risen +53 bps since the start of March when the Iran war began (Feb 28), effectively moving from pricing in rate cuts to now pricing in the possibility of a rate hike by late 2026. The long end rose less, reflecting competing forces between inflation fears and flight-to-safety demand amid Middle East turmoil.

**Treasury supply:** The government sold $606 billion in Treasury securities during the week (rollovers + new issuance), a massive supply event that contributed to upward yield pressure.

## Yield Curve

| Spread | Level (3/19-3/20) | Trend |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **2s10s** | +46 to +51 bps | Positively sloped; range of +52 to +74 bps over past 90 days |
| **3m10y** | +52 to +56 bps | Positive; no inversion signal |

**Curve shape:** The yield curve has solidly uninverted. The "sag" in the middle of the curve that persisted for three years has been eliminated. The curve is now normally sloped across all maturities, which represents a structural shift from the prolonged inversion regime of 2022-2025.

**Interpretation:** The steepening is being driven by the short end rising faster than the long end on hawkish Fed repricing, while the long end is anchored by competing forces (inflation premium pushing yields up, flight-to-safety from the Iran war pulling them down). The 2s10s spread narrowed slightly this week as the 2Y rose faster than the 10Y. This is a bear-flattening impulse within a broader steepening trend -- consistent with a market repricing toward "higher for longer" or even "hikes possible."

## Fed Watch

### FOMC Decision (March 18)

The FOMC voted **11-1 to hold** the federal funds rate at **3.50%-3.75%** for the second consecutive meeting. This was an SEP meeting with updated economic projections and dot plot.

**The lone dissent:** Governor Stephen Miran preferred a 25 bps cut, citing labor market deterioration. Miran has argued the Fed needs 150 bps of cuts within 2026 to align with what he believes is underlying inflation closer to 2%. Governor Waller, who dissented for a cut in January, voted to hold this time.

### March Dot Plot & SEP

| Projection | March 2026 | December 2025 | Change |
|------------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Fed Funds Rate (end 2026 median) | 3.4% | 3.4% | Unchanged |
| Fed Funds Rate (end 2027 median) | 3.00-3.25% | — | Low 3s |
| Core PCE (2026) | 2.7% | 2.5% | +20 bps |
| Headline PCE (2026) | 2.7% | 2.4% | +30 bps |
| Real GDP (2026) | 2.4% | 2.3% | +10 bps |

**Dot plot distribution:** Deeply divided. Seven members projected zero cuts in 2026, seven projected one cut. Two projected 50 bps of cuts, two projected 75 bps, and one projected 100 bps. The median held at one cut, but the center of gravity shifted hawkish -- more members moved from two cuts to one or zero.

### Powell Press Conference -- Key Quotes

- **On inflation:** "The forecast is that we will be making progress on inflation, not as much as we had hoped, but some progress on inflation."
- **On energy:** "Higher energy prices will push up overall inflation." Characterized inflationary pressure as "murky" and refused to rule out hikes if energy-driven costs bleed into sticky core services.
- **On the oil shock:** "The net of the oil shock will still be some downward pressure on spending and employment and upward pressure on inflation."
- **On stagflation:** "It's a very difficult situation, but it's nothing like what they faced in the 1970s and [I would] reserve 'stagflation' for that."
- **Conditional guidance:** "If we don't see that progress [on inflation], then you won't see that rate cut."
- **On his tenure:** Powell stated he has "no intention of leaving the board until the investigation is well and truly over with transparency and finality."

**Overall tone: Hawkish hold.** Powell pushed back hard on near-term rate cuts while leaving the door open to one cut if inflation cooperates. The market read was clearly hawkish -- equities sold off and short-end yields spiked.

### Post-Meeting Fed Speakers (March 19-21)

| Speaker | Date | Lean | Key Quote |
|---------|------|------|-----------|
| **Gov. Bowman** | 3/20 | Dovish | Penciled in **3 rate cuts** before year-end to "hopefully support the labor market." Called it "too soon to tell" on Iran's impact. |
| **Gov. Waller** | 3/20 | Cautious/Balanced | "I just want to wait and see where this goes." Open to advocating for cuts later if labor market weakens further. Warned: "If we get another 90,000 jobs decline in the next jobs report, that'll be like four negative reports out of five." Said he does **not** think there is a need to consider rate hikes. |

**Internal Fed tension is extreme.** Bowman wants 3 cuts (75 bps). The dot plot median is 1 cut (25 bps). Miran wants 150 bps of cuts. Seven members want zero cuts. The committee is as divided as it has been in years, reflecting the genuine macro uncertainty created by the Iran war's stagflationary shock.

## Credit Markets

### Spread Levels

| Index | OAS (bps) | Trend | Historical Context |
|-------|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| **IG Corporate (Master)** | ~85-90 bps | Widening from multi-decade tights (~78 bps at Q4 2025 close) | 2nd percentile over 20-year lookback at year-end; now moving toward 5th-10th percentile |
| **HY (High Yield)** | ~298-320 bps | Widening; moved from ~280 bps at month start | Still mid-range historically but repricing risk premium |
| **BB** | Tightening | Flight to quality within HY | |
| **CCC & Lower** | Widening | Weakest credits under pressure | |

### Market Dynamics

- **Spread widening accelerating:** The Iran war, hawkish Fed, and oil shock are driving a classic risk-off repricing in credit. Both IG and HY spreads have widened from the multi-decade tight levels that prevailed through Q4 2025 and into January.
- **Quality bifurcation:** BB-rated credits are seeing relative spread compression as investors rotate up in quality, while CCC and lower-rated names face meaningful widening. This is a textbook early-cycle deterioration pattern.
- **Supply pressure ahead:** Capital market forecasts for 2026 call for up to $2.25 trillion in IG issuance -- a 35% YoY increase. For the first time since 2022, supply may meaningfully outpace demand, creating further spread widening risk.
- **Energy sector exposure:** Credit markets are closely watching energy-exposed HY issuers. While higher oil prices benefit E&P companies, the broader economic slowdown risk from $100+ oil threatens the consumer and industrial sectors that dominate HY indices.

### Implications for Watchlist

Higher credit spreads and rising rates pressure capital-intensive, pre-revenue companies across our thematic sectors: CoreWeave (CRWV), QuantumScape (QS), Solid Power (SLDP), Lithium Americas (LAC), Rivian (RIVN), Oklo (OKLO), and NuScale (SMR) all face higher cost of capital. Grid storage projects (FLNC, EOSE) and EV charging buildouts (CHPT, EVGO) face extended payback periods. Companies with strong backlogs and contracted revenue (VRT, ETN, BWXT, FLNC) are relatively insulated.

## Liquidity

### TGA (Treasury General Account)

| Date | Balance |
|------|---------|
| 3/16/2026 | ~$938B |
| 3/13/2026 | ~$806B |
| 2/26/2026 | ~$849B |

The TGA has been volatile, swinging between ~$800B and ~$940B in March. Elevated TGA balances drain reserves from the banking system when Treasury issues debt and parks proceeds at the Fed. The $606B in Treasury issuance this week likely contributed to reserve volatility.

### ON RRP (Overnight Reverse Repo)

The ON RRP facility remains near **zero** -- effectively drained. This is a structural shift from the $2.5 trillion peak in 2023. With the ON RRP no longer acting as a liquidity buffer, every marginal liquidity shock (heavy Treasury issuance, quarter-end repositioning, foreign capital flows) falls directly on bank reserves. This increases funding market volatility risk, particularly around quarter-end (March 31).

### QT Status

**QT ended December 1, 2025.** The Fed stopped shrinking its balance sheet and is now reinvesting proceeds from maturing assets. The balance sheet was reduced from $8.9T (2022 peak) to ~$6.5T. The Fed began Reserve Management Purchases (RMPs) in December at up to $40B/month in short-term government debt to reduce funding market volatility. This effectively marks the transition from tightening to neutral on the balance sheet.

### Bank Reserves

With ON RRP drained, QT ended, and RMPs underway, the reserve picture is shifting toward adequacy concerns. The March quarter-end (March 31) will be the first major test of reserve adequacy in the post-QT, post-RRP environment. Watch for any spikes in overnight funding rates around month-end.

## Weekend Developments

### Iran War / Strait of Hormuz (Ongoing)

The most significant macro development remains the **2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis**, now in its third week since the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran began February 28. Key facts as of this weekend:

- **Oil prices:** Brent crude peaked near $126/bbl before easing to ~$100/bbl. WTI expected to average $98/bbl in Q2 2026 per the Dallas Fed.
- **Strait status:** Remains effectively closed. Over 150 tankers anchored outside. Major shipping lines (Maersk, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd) suspended transits.
- **LNG disruption:** QatarEnergy declared force majeure on all LNG shipments (March 4).
- **Gasoline:** U.S. average hit $3.54/gallon, up 17% since Feb 28.
- **IEA assessment:** Described this as the "greatest global energy security challenge in history."
- **Dallas Fed estimate:** Closure expected to lower global real GDP growth by an annualized 2.9 percentage points in Q2 2026.

- **Gov. Bowman (3/20):** Three cuts penciled in; defending policy independence.
- **Gov. Waller (3/20):** Cautious on oil/inflation; does not think hikes are needed; open to cuts later if labor market continues weakening.
- **J.P. Morgan (3/19):** Zero cuts in 2026, rate hike in 2027.
- **Morgan Stanley (3/19):** Pushed back cut forecasts on inflation fears.

This past week was a **key convergence**: FOMC SEP meeting (March 18) + Triple Witching (March 20) + S&P 500 quarterly rebalance (March 20). Per our calendar research, the post-Triple Witch week (March 23-27) historically shows weakness, and this is compounded by the AAPL buyback blackout beginning ~March 26. Expect continued volatility.
