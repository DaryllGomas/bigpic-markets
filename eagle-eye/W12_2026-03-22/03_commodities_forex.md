# Commodities & Forex — Week 12 (March 16-20, 2026)

*Eagle Eye Weekly Macro Report | Point-in-time snapshot as of Saturday, March 22, 2026*

---

This was a historic week for commodity and currency markets, dominated by the ongoing U.S.-Israel-Iran conflict and the near-total closure of the Strait of Hormuz (since March 4), the FOMC's hawkish hold on March 18, and a devastating flash crash in precious metals on March 19. The Strait of Hormuz crisis represents the largest disruption to global energy supply since the 1970s, with approximately 20 mb/d of crude and product flows effectively blocked.

## Energy

### Crude Oil

| Benchmark | Friday Close (Mar 20) | Prior Week Close (Mar 13) | Weekly Change | YTD Change |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|---------------|------------|
| WTI (CL1) | $98.32/bbl | ~$96.14/bbl | +$2.18 (+2.27%) | ~+78% |
| Brent (CO1) | $107.40/bbl | ~$108.78/bbl | ~-1.3% | ~+95% |

**Weekly narrative:** Crude oil prices remained extremely elevated but traded in a volatile, wide range. WTI traded between $93.42 and $99.67 during the week, while Brent ranged from approximately $107 to $114. The week opened with prices already elevated from the Strait of Hormuz closure (March 4-9 escalation). Brent hit a session peak of $126/bbl earlier in March before pulling back on partial reopening signals and SPR release announcements. Friday's rally was driven by Iraq declaring force majeure and Kuwait refineries coming under attack.

**OPEC+ developments:** On March 1, OPEC+ agreed to a 206,000 b/d output increase for April — a modest addition given that the Hormuz closure has removed approximately 20 mb/d from the market. Saudi Arabia led a 640,000 b/d output increase in March (the largest since June), accounting for about half the addition. The 12 OPEC members collectively pumped 29.52 mb/d. Despite the output increase, actual deliveries to global markets remain severely constrained by the Hormuz blockade.

**EIA inventory data (week ending March 13):** U.S. crude inventories rose by 6.2 million barrels to 449.3 million barrels, approximately 1% below the five-year average. This marked the third consecutive weekly build (following a 3.8 million barrel build the prior week). Refinery inputs averaged 16.2 mb/d at 91.4% utilization. Gasoline stocks fell 3.7 million barrels to 249.5 million; distillates fell 1.3 million barrels to 119.4 million.

**Key context:** Oil prices have surged approximately 95% over two months, from $55 in December 2025 to above $108 by late February/early March. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 4, with Iran making 21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships as of March 12. Iran has selectively allowed passage for some vessels (Indian-flagged carriers, Saudi tankers bound for India) while blocking U.S., Israeli, and Western-allied shipping.

## Metals

### Gold

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Friday Close (Mar 20) | ~$4,624/oz |
| Prior Week Close (Mar 13) | ~$5,050/oz (est.) |
| Weekly Change | approximately -8.5% |
| Session Low (Mar 19) | $4,557.80/oz |
| 2026 High | above $5,000/oz |

**Weekly narrative:** Gold suffered one of its worst weeks in modern history, losing close to 9%. The yellow metal had been consolidating near all-time highs above $5,000 through early March before a violent two-session selloff on March 18-19 saw it crash through the $5,000 barrier and extend to a session low of $4,557.80 on March 19.

**Flash crash mechanics (March 19):** A hot PPI print of 0.7% combined with the FOMC's hawkish hold (March 18, maintaining 3.50-3.75% with only one cut projected for 2026) triggered the initial break. When gold breached the $4,800 support level, a cascade of automated stop-loss orders created a liquidity vacuum — order book depth collapsed 98% between 9:01 AM and 9:30 AM ET. The DXY surging past 100 amplified the selling. Multi-asset margin calls across portfolios compounded the liquidation.

**Paradox:** Gold fell sharply during a major oil shock and geopolitical crisis — the opposite of its typical safe-haven behavior. This reflects the dominance of the dollar/rates channel: the Fed's hawkish stance and hot inflation data drove real rates expectations higher and the dollar stronger, overwhelming safe-haven demand. Forced liquidation to meet margin calls in energy positions also contributed.

## Weekend Developments (Post-Friday Close through March 22)

**CRITICAL — Trump issues 48-hour ultimatum to Iran:** On Saturday, March 22, President Trump stated the U.S. would "obliterate" Iran's power plants if Tehran did not fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz within 48 hours. This represents a significant escalation barely a day after he discussed "winding down" the war, now in its fourth week. The deadline implies a potential military strike as early as Monday, March 24.

**Oil prices rising in weekend trading:** Brent crude futures rose to $113.20/bbl (+0.90%) and WTI to $98.85/bbl (+0.63%) in early weekend electronic trading, after settling at the highest levels since July 2022 on Friday.

**C-suite "two-week deadline":** CNBC reported that corporate executives and traders see approximately two weeks as the maximum window before the Hormuz closure triggers a second-order economic crisis — sustained $120+ oil would sharply increase recession odds globally.

**Iran diplomatic signals:** Earlier in March, Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi indicated Tehran is open to talks with countries wanting safe passage through the strait, but no concrete ceasefire progress has materialized as of Saturday evening.

**FOMC implications:** The Fed's March 18 hawkish hold — with inflation projections raised to 2.7% for 2026 and only one rate cut projected — creates a difficult backdrop. If oil remains above $100, inflation expectations will continue to rise, potentially forcing the Fed to maintain restrictive policy longer even as growth slows. The stagflation risk is now the dominant macro concern.

**Monday risk:** Markets face extreme event risk at the open on Monday, March 23. The 48-hour Trump ultimatum, ongoing Hormuz disruption, and post-triple-witching (March 20) positioning unwind could amplify volatility. The calendar notes that the post-triple-witch week (March 23-27) historically exhibits weakness.
