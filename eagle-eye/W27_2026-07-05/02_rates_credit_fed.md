Computed from the July 2, 2026 closes above:

- **2s10s: +35 bps** (10Y 4.49% − 2Y 4.14%). Positive but historically narrow; a third-party tracker put the spread near +0.28% in early July. Well below the +100–150 bp range typical of a healthy expansion.

- **Shape:** The curve is *positively sloped but flat/compressed*. It emerged from the 2022–2024 inversion but a hawkish front end (funds at 3.50–3.75% with a hike bias) is capping bull-steepening. Classic **late-cycle** signature: elevated front end, flat belly, long end lifted by term-premium/inflation concerns. The modest June-to-July steepening reflects a higher 30Y (~4.99%) more than a lower front end. ([centralbank.watch US yield curve](https://centralbank.watch/tools/yield-curve/us-yield-curve/))

## Fed Watch

**Policy setting:** Target range **3.50%–3.75%** (effective fed funds ~**3.63%**), **held** at the June 16–17, 2026 FOMC — the first meeting chaired by **Kevin Warsh**. ([June 17 FOMC statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260617a.htm); [Fox Business, 6/17/26](https://www.foxbusiness.com/economy/federal-reserve-interest-rate-decision-june-17-2026))

**The June 17, 2026 dot plot flipped hawkish (the big shift):**
- Median year-end 2026 funds rate raised to **3.8%** (from a March median that still implied a *cut*) — i.e., a hike is now the median base case. Of 18 participants: 9 at/below the current 3.50–3.75%, 3 saw one 25 bp hike, 5 saw ~50 bp of hikes. **17 of 18 see inflation risks tilted to the upside.**
- Inflation projections **raised**: 2026 headline PCE **3.6%**, core **3.3%**. GDP trimmed to **2.2%**; unemployment **4.3%**; long-run rate held at **3.1%**.
- Sources: [FOMC SEP, June 17, 2026](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20260617.htm); [CNBC, 6/17/26](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/17/fed-interest-rate-decision-june-2026.html); [StockTitan, 6/17/26](https://www.stocktitan.net/articles/fed-rate-decision-june-17-2026).

**Warsh's "new course":** He **removed traditional forward guidance** in favor of pure data dependence and **declined to submit his own SEP dot**. At Sintra (7/1) he stressed inflation is "too high," while acknowledging the Fed is more open to AI as a potential disinflationary force. ([CNBC ECB forum, 7/1/26](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/01/kevin-warsh-ecb-forum-live-updates.html); [CNBC "new course," 7/1/26](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/01/warsh-faces-multiple-alternative-inflation-signs-as-fed-charts-new-course.html))

**Market-implied path (CME FedWatch, as of July 4, 2026):**
- **July 29, 2026 meeting: ~75.6% hold**, remainder skewed toward a *hike* (not a cut).
- After the soft June jobs report, the implied probability of a **September hike fell to ~55% (from ~64%)**.
- Futures broadly price the funds rate rising toward **~3.8% by October and ~4% by year-end 2026**, holding near 4% into mid-2027.
- Source: [CME FedWatch Tool](https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/interest-rates/cme-fedwatch-tool.html); [CNBC, 7/2/26](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/02/us-treasury-yields-rise-as-investors-await-june-jobs-report.html).

## Credit Markets

Credit remains conspicuously **calm/tight** despite the hawkish rates backdrop (data are the most recent available; corporate OAS series lag the holiday):

- **US HY OAS (ICE BofA): ~2.63%–2.78% (263–278 bps), June 2026.** Near historic tights — the series' all-time low is ~2.41% and its long-run median is ~4.52%. ([FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLH0A0HYM2); [GuruFocus, Jun 2026](https://www.gurufocus.com/economic_indicators/5735/bofa-us-high-yield-index-optionadjusted-spread))
- **US IG Corporate OAS (ICE BofA): ~77 bps (as of 5/12/26); ~80 bps early 2026.** BBB ~100 bps, AA ~50 bps — all historically tight. ([FRED BAMLC0A0CM](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLC0A0CM))
- **Read-through:** Spreads this tight say the market is pricing benign corporate credit and no imminent recession, even as the front-end curve inverts on a hike bias. All-in yields (Treasury + spread) remain attractive to buyers, sustaining demand. The risk is asymmetric: with HY OAS <300 bps, there is little cushion if the hawkish Fed / tariff-inflation mix eventually bites growth.
- **Bank lending (SLOOS, April 2026 survey, covering Q1 2026):** modest net share of banks **tightened C&I standards** with demand roughly unchanged; higher premiums on riskier loans, tighter covenants/collateral. Consumer standards mostly unchanged. Banks expect standards "basically unchanged" across 2026. The July 2026 SLOOS (Q2) is not yet out. ([Fed April 2026 SLOOS](https://www.federalreserve.gov/data/sloos/sloos-202604.htm))

## Liquidity

- **TGA (Treasury General Account): ~$770.6B as of July 2, 2026** (down $36.8B on the day). The balance is **elevated** — Treasury has rebuilt cash post-debt-ceiling — which drains reserves from the system as it fills. ([IndexBox, 7/2/26](https://www.indexbox.io/blog/us-treasury-general-account-balance-declines-on-july-2-2026/))
- **ON RRP: drained to a de-minimis level (roughly under ~$100–200B) by mid-2026**, from a ~$2.5T peak in late 2022. Money-market funds long ago rotated into T-bills. The ON RRP "shock absorber" is essentially exhausted. ([PreReason RRP 2026](https://www.prereason.com/insights/reverse-repo); [FRED RRPONTSYD](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD))
- **Net liquidity picture:** With ON RRP near zero and the TGA elevated/rebuilding, further Treasury cash builds now pull directly on **bank reserves**. Reserves are still characterized as "ample," but the buffer is thinning — a factor to watch for money-market/repo pressure into quarter-end and tax dates. The Standing Repo Facility remains the backstop.

## Weekend Developments (since Thu 7/2 close through 7/5)

US cash bond and stock markets were **closed Fri July 3** and over the weekend (reopen Mon July 6), so there was no new price action — but several catalysts landed just before/into the break:

1. **Warsh's hawkish Sintra remarks (Wed 7/1)** and the **soft June jobs report (Thu 7/2, +57k)** are the freshest inputs carried into the long weekend. Net message: the Fed retains a hike bias, but labor softening buys time — markets lean "hold" for July 29.
2. **Geopolitics / oil — de-escalation continuing.** Following the Middle East conflict that erupted in late February 2026, **crude has fallen to roughly $67–69/bbl** (near pre-war levels) as Strait of Hormuz flows recovered above 10M bbl/day and US–Iran peace talks progressed in Doha. Talks face a delay around the **funeral of Iran's former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, beginning July 4**. Lower oil is a **disinflationary tailwind** that partly offsets tariff-driven goods inflation — relevant to how hard the Warsh Fed leans hawkish. ([TradingEconomics crude oil](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/crude-oil))
3. **Tariff cliff — a live rates/inflation catalyst.** The temporary **10% blanket tariff under Section 122** of the Trade Act of 1974 is scheduled to **expire July 24, 2026**; the US Court of International Trade ruled on **May 7, 2026** that the administration exceeded its Section 122 authority. The outcome swings the inflation path the Fed is reacting to. ([Investing.com July outlook](https://www.investing.com/analysis/july-market-outlook-tariffs-trade-and-the-next-inflation-test-200682939); [Tax Foundation tariff tracker](https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/))
4. **No new Fed policy action over the holiday itself** — the next scheduled events are the July 8 FOMC minutes and the July 29 decision.
