## This Week's Take

The week's defining tension is a **soft jobs print landing on top of a structurally hawkish Fed**. June nonfarm payrolls grew just **+57,000** (vs. ~115k expected), with April/May revised down a combined 74k and the unemployment-rate dip to 4.2% flattered by a fall in participation to 61.5% — the weakest labor read in months. Crucially, in 2026 the policy debate is about **hikes, not cuts**: new Fed Chair **Kevin Warsh** (in office since May) chaired his first meeting June 16–17 and the dot plot **flipped to imply a hike** (median 2026 dot 3.8% vs. the current 3.50–3.75% range; 17 of 18 officials see inflation risk to the upside). So the weak jobs number was read as *dovish-at-the-margin within a hawkish regime* — it trimmed September **hike** odds to ~50–55% (from ~65%), capped the dollar, and sent **gold to $4,170** for its first weekly gain in about a month. That single distinction — a softer print lowering *hike* risk rather than raising *cut* odds — is the through-line connecting every asset class this week.

Underneath the macro, the tape was all **rotation**. The **Dow printed a fresh record (52,900, +1.14%) on July 2** while the **Nasdaq-100 fell 1.6%, the Russell 2000 dropped >1%, and Tesla sank ~7.5%** despite a blowout Q2 delivery beat (480,126, +25% YoY) — a sell-the-news, margin-doubt reaction. That one-day divergence is the microcosm of H1 2026's big story: capital broadening out of concentrated mega-cap tech into industrials, financials and small-caps (Russell 2000 +22.6% YTD, its best first half in ~35 years). Semiconductors extended their slide into the break, and "Big Short" investor **Michael Burry disclosed fresh shorts (June 30)** against NVDA, MU, TSLA, SOXX, AMAT and CAT, calling the AI-chip boom "the beginning of the end." Yet VIX at **16.59** and credit spreads near historic tights (HY OAS ~2.7%) say the broad market remains complacent.

**Into Monday's open, the top discrete weekend catalyst is OPEC+.** On Sunday, July 5, seven producers agreed to add **+188,000 b/d in August** — the latest in a run of monthly hikes unwinding the 2023 cuts — piling supply onto a crude tape that already fell a third straight week to its lowest since late February as the Strait of Hormuz war premium fully round-tripped (WTI ~$68.8, Brent low-$70s). The two-way risk is a July 4 flare-up in the **Hormuz "transit fee" dispute** between Iran and Washington. Beyond energy, this was a *quiet* geopolitical weekend — no new war, crisis, or shock — with the Iran conflict still under its June ceasefire. Bias: mildly bearish energy, benign-to-constructive for the broad tape, with S&P futures holding Friday's gain (~62% implied odds of a higher Monday open).

## Weekend & Breaking Developments (since Thu 7/2 close → Sun 7/5)

1. **OPEC+ adds 188k b/d for August (decided online Sunday, July 5).** Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria and Oman agreed to raise collective output by **+188,000 b/d in August 2026**, continuing the monthly unwind of the April-2023 voluntary cuts; the group keeps meeting monthly and could pause or reverse. **Bearish-at-the-margin for oil/energy equities into Monday**, layered on an already-soft crude tape. *(Confirmed: IndexBox, Muscat Daily, Nairametrics — all 7/5/26.)*

This is the key two-way oil risk: continued normalization is bearish; a punitive fee regime or re-escalation is bullish.

Open interest sits at a 30-day high into Monday, a cascading-liquidation risk in either direction. (BTC ~$62,800, ETH ~$1,764 as of Sunday.)
4. **CLARITY Act (crypto market structure) blocked/delayed in the Senate before July 4.** Sticking points: DeFi/AML, stablecoin oversight, jurisdiction and Section 604 law-enforcement objections. Emergency Senate meetings are expected the week of July 6; analysts say it likely needs to clear the Senate by end-July to pass in 2026. 5. **No new major geopolitical or corporate shock over the weekend.** The late-February Iran conflict remains under an uneasy ceasefire; a widely circulated July 4 CoinDesk "$70B Aptos flaw" story is a **patched February vulnerability, no funds lost** — historical context, not a fresh hack.
