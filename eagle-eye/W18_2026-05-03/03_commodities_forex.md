# Commodities & Forex — Week Ending May 1, 2026

## Energy

### Crude Oil

The dominant market story of 2026 continues to be the 2026 Iran war and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz — a disruption the IEA has characterized as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Oil flows through the strait have been largely blocked since March 4, cutting off approximately 20% of global oil supplies and stranding Iranian, Qatari, and Persian Gulf crude and LNG exports. The EIA estimates the Persian Gulf shut in roughly 9.1 million barrels per day in April.

**WTI crude** closed Friday May 1 at **$101.94/bbl**, down nearly 3% on the day but still up significantly week-over-week. WTI had briefly traded near $106 earlier in the week amid fears of a prolonged Hormuz closure, before retreating as ceasefire optimism — reports of a fragile US-Iran truce — entered the market. WTI prices have surged roughly 60% since the conflict began on February 28. The weekly gain was the second consecutive weekly advance.

**Brent crude** settled at **$108.17/bbl** on May 1, down roughly 2% on the day from a prior close of $110.40. Earlier in the week, Brent had traded above $116 intraday as siege-of-Iran-ports fears peaked. The EIA's own Q2 2026 forecast calls for Brent averaging approximately $115/bbl; Goldman Sachs expects Brent to stay well above $100 for the foreseeable future given persistent supply losses.

The weekly price action reflected a tug-of-war: supply shock from Hormuz closure (bullish) vs. ceasefire optimism and some demand-destruction fears from energy-price-induced inflation (bearish). On Sunday May 3, oil futures slipped further to approximately $100.69 in electronic/overnight trading as ceasefire hopes persisted.

**Natural gas (Henry Hub)** settled at **$2.78/MMBtu** on May 1, up 0.47% on the day but down 23% year-over-year. Domestically, bearish fundamentals dominate: storage levels are estimated 8% above seasonal norms for the week ending April 24, up from 7% the prior week. Production has fallen ~4.1 bcfd over the past 18 days to an 11-week low of 108.1 bcfd, as producers including EQT scaled back in response to low prices. Qatar's force majeure declaration on LNG exports is a geopolitical wildcard for global gas markets, but US domestic Henry Hub remains oversupplied in the near term.

### OPEC+ Weekend Decision (May 3, 2026 — Breaking)

In the first OPEC+ production decision since the UAE's formal departure from the cartel (effective May 1, 2026), the remaining seven OPEC+ members — Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman — agreed on Sunday May 3 to implement a production increase of **188,000 barrels per day** from previously-announced voluntary adjustment cuts. The increase is effective June 2026, with a follow-up meeting scheduled for June.

The decision is largely symbolic in the near term: with the Strait of Hormuz still effectively blocked, incremental OPEC+ barrels have limited ability to reach global markets through the Persian Gulf. However, the move signals the cartel's intent to gradually normalize output and signals member discipline ahead of a potential Hormuz reopening. The UAE's departure removes approximately 3.0–3.2 mb/d of production capacity from OPEC+ coordination going forward, and gives Abu Dhabi flexibility to pursue its own output targets.

### EIA Inventory Data

The most recent EIA weekly petroleum report (week ending April 24, released April 29) showed:
- US crude inventories **fell by 6.233 million barrels** to **459.5 million barrels**, well above the consensus expectation of a 0.2 million barrel draw.
- The prior week (ending April 17) saw a 1.925 million barrel build to 465.7 million barrels.

The larger-than-expected draw reflects a meaningful acceleration in US crude exports as global buyers shift to US supply to compensate for disrupted Persian Gulf flows. US crude exports have reportedly surged to record levels.

## Metals

### Gold

Spot gold closed the week at approximately **$4,568.82/oz** on May 1, down 1.1% on the day and tracking toward a weekly loss of approximately 1.2%. Intraday on May 1, gold had traded near $4,592 before selling off. The metal's primary headwind this week was the inverse relationship between elevated oil prices and gold: sky-high energy costs are fanning inflation concerns, suppressing market expectations for Fed rate cuts, and thus reducing gold's relative attractiveness as a non-yielding asset.

The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.50%–3.75% on Wednesday (April 29), as expected, with CME FedWatch showing near-100% probability of a hold. US 10-year Treasury yields hovered around 4.3%–4.4% through the week, limiting gold's upside. One supportive factor was a sharp dollar depreciation on Thursday/Friday following suspected Bank of Japan FX intervention, which provided a partial offset.

Gold remains up dramatically year-over-year — approximately $1,351/oz or ~40%+ versus a year ago — reflecting the massive geopolitical risk premium embedded in precious metals since the Iran war began. The $5,400 January 28, 2026 record close has become near-term resistance.

### Silver

Silver closed at **$75.16/oz** on May 1, up 1.98% on the day — notably outperforming gold. Silver's dual role as a monetary hedge and an industrial metal (solar panels, electronics) has kept it better supported than gold during the inflation-driven selloff. Silver is up approximately 135% year-over-year, reflecting both the Iran war safe-haven bid and structural industrial demand growth.

### Copper

Copper futures steadied near **$5.95/lb** ($13,115/tonne) on May 1, following four consecutive sessions of declines, with a slight -0.27% day-over-day close. The week's weakness reflects a complex cross-current: (1) Hormuz disruption fears weighing on global growth expectations and thus industrial metals demand; (2) offsetting support from China demand resilience and data-center-driven supply agreement activity; (3) a supply-side complication — the Iran war's disruption of sulphur shipments to China is curtailing sulphuric acid exports, a critical input for approximately half of Chile's copper refining operations.

The portfolio thesis remains intact: ICSG projects a 150,000-tonne supply deficit in 2026, with J.P. Morgan estimating up to 330,000 MT. The Freeport-McMoRan Grasberg (Indonesia) phased restart in Q2 2026 is the key near-term catalyst to watch. Despite the week's softness, copper at ~$5.95/lb reflects meaningful structural demand premium from AI data center buildout and electrification.

### Uranium

Uranium spot settled at **$86.55/lb U3O8** as of May 1–2, down 0.35% on the day. Over the past month, uranium has risen 1.64%, and is up approximately **24% year-over-year**, reflecting the ongoing structural supply deficit (30–40 million lb annual gap) and surging nuclear utility demand driven by hyperscaler AI data center power contracts. Kazatomprom continues to operate below 100% capacity, providing price discipline. The geopolitical disruption of Persian Gulf LNG is further reinforcing the attractiveness of nuclear as a non-interruptible energy source, potentially accelerating utility contracting.

### Platinum Group Metals

**Palladium** rose to approximately **$1,546–1,556/oz** on May 1, up 0.82% on the day and up approximately 63% year-over-year — the largest YoY gain among precious metals. The Iran war's disruption to global automotive supply chains (tightening ICE vehicle production) combined with Russian supply constraints (Russia = ~40% of palladium supply) is driving an atypical bull run. **Platinum** was trading in the range of approximately $2,730–$3,018/oz for May 2026, according to available forecasts, though exact closing data was unavailable at report time.

## Critical Minerals

### Rare Earth Elements (NdPr, Heavy REEs)

NdPr prices have experienced a dramatic and sustained surge in 2026 driven by China's April 2025 export licensing regime on seven heavy rare earth elements (terbium, dysprosium, gadolinium, samarium, lutetium, scandium, yttrium), which remains in force. China's domestic index (PrNd oxide) is tracking approximately **$34–$37/kg**, while quoted metal prices outside China have reached approximately **$126/kg** for NdPr alloy (up ~138% from the start of 2026 vs. ~$53/kg in January). Outside China, quoted prices have reportedly reached ~$220/kg, reflecting a significant and growing East-West pricing differential.

Dysprosium is approximately **$190/kg** and terbium exceeds **$700/kg** at China's domestic index levels. The October 2025 most expansive controls (extraterritorial provisions) remain suspended through November 2026, but the April 2025 licensing architecture is fully operational. The Rare Earth Exchanges noted in its April 27–May 1 deal roundup that China is engaged in "quiet tightening" — strategic management of export licensing pace — even within the suspension window.

For portfolio names: MP Materials (MP) and Lynas (LYC.AX) are the direct beneficiaries of ex-China NdPr price elevation. Energy Fuels (UUUU) remains on track for commercial Dy/Tb production by Q4 2026. USA Rare Earth (USAR) continues to ramp toward its first 1,200 t/year magnet line under $1.6B CHIPS Act backing.

### Lithium

Lithium carbonate prices are currently trading in the **$18,000–$22,000/tonne range** (spot above $20,000 in early 2026 data, with some reports indicating ~$18,500 stabilization). The 2024 "Lithium Winter" lows below $10,000/tonne appear to be a confirmed bottom. The supply-demand picture is tightening toward near-balance in 2026 (Fastmarkets projects a 1,500 tonne deficit; S&P narrowing surplus to ~109,000 MT). Key producers — Albemarle (ALB), SQM (SQM), Pilbara Minerals (PLS.AX) — are watching whether the $20,000 level holds as a floor to trigger margin expansion. Thacker Pass (LAC) construction is progressing toward late-2027 mechanical completion.

### Cobalt

Cobalt metal was trading near **$28.15/lb** ($62,100/tonne) as of early May 2026, up approximately 6.63% since the start of 2026, and well above the ~$21,500/tonne level before the DRC's February 2025 export controls. The DRC's 2026 annual quota is set at approximately 96,600 tonnes (87,000 tonnes distributed pro rata to producers, 9,600 tonnes under ARECOMS discretion), with the DRC retaining the right to adjust quarterly. Glencore expects DRC cobalt exports to normalize broadly in line with 2026 quotas. Cobalt entered 2026 around $56,414/tonne and has pulled back from those peak levels but remains structurally supported. The DRC quota regime has shifted the market from oversupply to projected deficit.

### Nickel

Nickel faces persistent structural oversupply from Indonesian production (~212,000–288,000 tonne surplus through 2026 per thesis estimates). No notable price catalysts emerged this week. Nickel-exposed names remain under pressure until Indonesian production discipline materializes.

## Forex

### US Dollar (DXY)

The US Dollar Index (DXY) closed Friday May 1 near **98**, having declined approximately 0.62% for the week and hitting its lowest level since late February. The primary driver was a sharp, suspected Bank of Japan intervention in currency markets on Thursday (April 30) that sent the yen surging more than 2–3%, which by construction drove significant DXY weakness (JPY = ~13.6% DXY weight).

The dollar remains caught between two opposing forces: elevated US inflation from $100+ oil (would normally support rates and USD) vs. a Fed that is holding rates steady at 3.50%–3.75% while the global economy absorbs an unprecedented supply shock. The dollar's multi-week downtrend from the 99–100 area to sub-98 reflects some deterioration in US economic exceptionalism narratives as energy-price inflation broadens.

### Major Pairs

**EUR/USD** closed at approximately **1.1717** on May 1, down 0.09% on the session. EUR/USD has been broadly supported by dollar weakness but faces headwinds from European energy vulnerability — the EU is highly exposed to disrupted LNG/oil flows from the Persian Gulf and Qatar. The ECB is navigating an economy where energy inflation complicates the rate trajectory.

**USD/JPY** closed near **157** on May 1, having surged as much as 3% intraday (touching 155.5) before settling. The yen had weakened past 160 per dollar on Wednesday April 29 — its lowest since July 2024, when Japan last intervened. Japanese Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama delivered a "final" warning ahead of Thursday's intervention, which the Ministry of Finance has not formally confirmed but markets universally attributed to government action. Japan's sensitivity: the Iran war's energy shock hits Japan with unusual severity given its near-total dependence on imported energy, creating a stagflationary yen-weakening dynamic that BOJ and MoF are fighting.

**GBP/USD** closed at approximately **1.3650**, up 0.38% on May 1, carried higher by broad dollar weakness and relatively better UK energy positioning versus continental Europe.

### Emerging Markets

EM currencies consolidated recent gains in the week ending May 1. The VanEck J.P. Morgan EM Local Currency Bond ETF (EMLC) was roughly flat year-to-date but off approximately 1.7% in the past week, reflecting some EM-specific caution as the Iran war's inflationary spillovers create divergent central bank outlooks. Dollar weakness has been a tailwind for EM currencies broadly (EM real yields continue to exceed DM levels), but oil-importing EMs (India, Turkey, Philippines) face intensifying current account pressure from $100+ crude. Oil-exporting EMs (Saudi Arabia, GCC) are booking windfall revenues — Bloomberg noted Saudi Arabia is "set for an oil windfall" from Hormuz-driven prices.

Brent has pulled back from the $110+ area to the low $108s. The ceasefire narrative is fragile: Iran reimposed restrictions after a brief partial opening in mid-April; oil flow resumption through Hormuz is assumed gradual through May-June.

**No Weekend Forex Developments:** Major FX markets are closed on weekends; the BOJ intervention's aftershocks are expected to continue shaping USD/JPY trading in early Asian hours Monday. No additional major central bank actions were announced.

**Geopolitical Watch:** The 2026 Iran war remains the single most important commodity macro variable. The IEA describes this as the "greatest global energy security challenge in history." The path of Hormuz reopening — and pace of Persian Gulf supply normalization — will drive virtually all energy and related commodity price action through Q2–Q3 2026.
