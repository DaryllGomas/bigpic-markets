## Yield Curve

**2s10s Spread:** +47 bps (3.88% vs. 4.35%) — positively sloped, steepened modestly on the week as the 10-year fell faster than the front end.

**3m10y Spread:** Approximate data unavailable for exact May 1 print. The curve has been normalizing from the deep 2022-2024 inversion (which reached −108 bps at its worst). The re-steepening of the 2s10s into positive territory — now running around +45–50 bps — reflects the classic post-inversion pattern: short rates anchored by a Fed on hold while long rates are pulled by inflation risk premium (energy shock) and Warsh transition uncertainty.

**Shape and Signal:** The curve is bear-steepening at the long end. The 30-year at ~4.97% is pricing in:
1. Structurally higher-for-longer inflation expectations (Iran/oil shock; OECD now forecasts US CPI at 4.2% in 2026, up 120 bps from prior forecast)
2. Anticipated acceleration of Fed balance sheet reduction under Warsh (market pricing in more supply at the long end)
3. Elevated term premium as fiscal deficit trajectory remains unresolved (~$39T in federal debt, $1T+ annual interest costs per Dimon)

The positive 2s10s spread is not yet a recession signal in isolation — it reflects an ongoing post-inversion normalization rather than a growth acceleration.

## Fed Watch

### FOMC Meeting — April 28–29, 2026 (Decision: April 29)

**Decision:** Hold. Fed Funds target range maintained at **3.50%–3.75%** for the third consecutive meeting.

**Vote:** **8–4** — the most dissents since October 1992.

| Dissenter | Direction | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Beth Hammack (Cleveland) | Hawkish | Objected to "easing bias" in statement; Iran war stoking broad-based inflation |
| Neel Kashkari (Minneapolis) | Hawkish | Said statement contained inappropriate forward guidance; next move "could be a cut or a hike" |
| Lorie Logan (Dallas) | Hawkish | "Increasingly concerned" about inflation; Middle East raises prospect of repeated supply disruptions |
| Stephen Miran (Trump appointee) | Dovish | Favored an immediate rate cut; sixth consecutive dissent toward easier policy |

**Statement language:** The majority retained an "easing bias," signaling the next move is still likely a cut — but the three hawkish dissenters openly rejected this framing. The language is increasingly contentious within the FOMC.

### Powell's Final Meeting as Chair

April 29 was Jerome Powell's **last press conference as Fed Chair** before his term expires May 15, 2026. Key Powell quotes:
- *"Developments in the Middle East are contributing to a high level of uncertainty about the economic outlook."*
- *"We're committed to bring inflation back down to two percent, and sustainably... we'll stick at it until that happens."*
- *"The oil-driven inflation shock hasn't peaked yet."*
- On Trump's legal attacks: Trump DOJ dropped its criminal investigation of Powell on April 29 (reportedly the condition that led Sen. Thom Tillis to commit his vote for Warsh). Powell confirmed he will **remain on the Board of Governors** (term runs through January 2028) but pledged not to act as a "shadow chair."

### Warsh Succession

- Senate Banking Committee voted **13–11 along party lines** on April 29 to advance Kevin Warsh's nomination.
- Full Senate vote expected the **week of May 11** after recess; Powell's chair term expires **May 15**.
- Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA) has signaled support, providing potential bipartisan cover.
- Warsh is expected to chair the **June 16–17 FOMC** (SEP meeting with dot plot).

## Liquidity

### Treasury General Account (TGA)

- **Balance:** Specific May 1 balance data unavailable from search results; last confirmed public reference was in the $600–800B range range consistent with recent quarters. The Daily Treasury Statement (updated April 30, 2026) is the authoritative source.
- The TGA remains a key variable: large drawdowns inject reserves into the banking system and can compress short-term rates; rebuilding after debt-ceiling episodes creates the opposite drag.

### ON RRP (Overnight Reverse Repo)

- The Fed's ON RRP facility has been effectively **drained to near-zero** following the 2022–2024 drawdown cycle. The facility once absorbed $2.5T+ at its 2022 peak.
- With ON RRP essentially exhausted, the remaining buffer between ample and scarce reserves has narrowed. Repo rates now respond more quickly to TGA swings, and the Fed has less passive stabilization capacity.
- QT (quantitative tightening) continues to slowly withdraw reserves, compounding the tightness at the margin.

### Bank Reserves & Repo

- Bank reserve conditions: Trending toward "less ample" as QT proceeds with ON RRP buffer gone. Specific reserve balance for week of May 1 unavailable.
- No notable repo market stress events reported for the week.

### Financial Conditions Index (FCI)

- Global FCI tightened approximately **14 bps** in early March 2026 following initial Iran war escalation
- Chicago Fed NFCI: specific week-ending May 1 print data unavailable; directionally conditions remain elevated versus early-2026 levels on oil/geopolitical risk
- Warsh's anticipated balance sheet acceleration (once confirmed) is expected to drive further tightening of FCIs through yield channel

## Weekend Developments

**Substantial developments as of Sunday May 3, 2026:**

1. **Warsh transition framing dominates.** Multiple outlets (CNBC, Motley Fool) published weekend pieces on what Warsh's arrival means for fixed income. The core thesis: Warsh wants to (a) shrink the Fed's balance sheet aggressively, (b) eliminate forward rate guidance, and (c) restore institutional credibility. The bond market implication is higher long-end yields as Treasury supply grows and the Fed steps back from its implicit support role. CNBC May 2 headline: *"Why Kevin Warsh's arrival at the Fed may catch fixed-income investors off guard."*

2. **Three hawkish dissenters (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan) publicly explained their dissent on May 1,** explicitly stating the next rate move "could be either a cut or a hike." This language — from three sitting regional Fed presidents — represents a material hawkish signal beyond what is priced in futures markets. TheStreet noted this as an "alarming shift" in the rate outlook.

3. **Iran peace signal / oil retreat.** Late Friday and Saturday reports indicate Iran sent a ceasefire proposal, causing oil prices to ease. This is a live geopolitical variable that will shape Monday's Treasury open — if confirmed, could allow the 10-year to extend its pullback from the 4.45% mid-week high.

4. **Dimon bond crisis warning lingers.** JPMorgan CEO's April 28–May 2 commentary on systemic fiscal risk and a potential bond crisis was still circulating over the weekend. Not an immediate market catalyst but reinforces the term premium narrative.

5. **Senate recess through May 11.** The Senate is in recess until the week of May 11, when the full Warsh confirmation vote is expected. No policy action possible until then. Powell officially steps down as chair on **May 15**.
