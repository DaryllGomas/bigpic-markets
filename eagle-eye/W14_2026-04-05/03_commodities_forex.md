# Commodities & Forex — Week 14 (Week Ending April 3, 2026)

*Point-in-time snapshot as of Saturday, April 5, 2026*

---

## Energy

### Crude Oil

The dominant story across all commodity markets is the 2026 Iran war and Strait of Hormuz crisis, now in its sixth week. The Strait — through which ~20% of global oil supply normally transits — remains effectively closed, with Iran permitting selective passage only to ships from China, Russia, India, Iraq, and Pakistan. Maritime traffic is down over 90% from normal levels.

**WTI Crude** settled at **$111.54/bbl** on Friday April 3, up **$11.90 (+11.94%)** on the holiday-shortened week. Intraday WTI traded as high as $114.18 on Thursday. **Brent Crude** closed at approximately **$112.42/bbl** on Friday morning, up from ~$104.86 on April 1. Brent gained roughly $7.50-8.00 on the week.

For context, WTI first breached $100 on March 27 (its first time above that level since 2022). At the start of March, Brent was around $94. Oil is up roughly 50% year-to-date.

**EIA Inventories:** The April 1 weekly petroleum status report (for week ending March 27) showed a **+5.451M barrel** build in crude stocks, well above the +1.8M barrel consensus. The prior week showed a +6.926M build. Builds reflect SPR releases and demand destruction from high prices, partially offsetting supply disruption.

**OPEC+:** On March 1, OPEC+ agreed to a **+206,000 b/d** production increase for April, led by Saudi Arabia (+62k), Russia (+62k), Iraq (+26k), and the UAE (+18k). However, the increase is largely symbolic — logistical and geopolitical constraints (Hormuz closure, Iranian drone attacks on Gulf infrastructure) limit actual delivery capacity. OPEC+ met again today (April 5) and **approved in principle** a similar paper increase for May, while acknowledging that physical supply remains constrained by the conflict.

### Natural Gas

**Henry Hub** was trading at approximately **$2.87/MMBtu** as of April 1, well below the EIA's full-year 2026 forecast of ~$3.80/MMBtu. The 2026 injection season started early: the EIA reported a **+36 Bcf** injection for the week ending March 27, bringing working gas to **1,865 Bcf** — roughly two weeks ahead of the 5-year average seasonal start.

Domestically, mild March weather and the early injection season are keeping Henry Hub prices subdued despite the global energy crisis. The disconnect between US natural gas (abundant, domestically produced) and global LNG markets is stark.

**European TTF** is a different story entirely. Dutch TTF was trading at **~EUR 50/MWh** as of April 2, having spiked to nearly EUR 70/MWh in early March when the Iran war began. The 52-week range is EUR 26.55 - EUR 69.35. Europe gets 12-14% of its LNG from Qatar (transiting Hormuz), and started 2026 with much lower gas storage (46 bcm at end-February vs. 60 bcm in 2025 and 77 bcm in 2024). The EU estimates gas prices have risen 70% and oil 50% from the crisis, adding an extra EUR 13 billion to fossil fuel import bills. Some European countries (Slovenia) have introduced fuel rationing; others (Austria) are cutting fuel taxes.

**Thesis connection:** The Hormuz crisis dramatically validates the natural-gas-as-bridge thesis for data center power (AI thesis). Williams Companies ($5.1B committed) and Kinder Morgan (40% of US gas) benefit from the structural separation between US domestic gas supply and global LNG pricing. The nuclear renaissance thesis is also reinforced — 24/7 baseload power independent of fossil fuel supply chains becomes more attractive as geopolitical energy disruption risk reprices upward.

## Metals

### Gold

Gold closed Friday April 3 at approximately **$4,677/oz**, down **$81.88 (-1.72%)** on the day, after hitting an intraday high of $4,796 earlier in the session. The opening price was $4,643.

On a weekly basis, gold's performance has been paradoxically weak given the geopolitical crisis. March 2026 was gold's **worst month since October 2008**, declining **14.6%**. The driver is a strengthening US dollar and rising interest rate expectations — strong US jobs data reinforced expectations the Fed will keep rates higher for longer, and the dollar's haven bid has dominated gold's haven bid in this particular crisis.

Gold remains up substantially year-to-date and is near all-time highs in absolute terms at ~$4,700, but the sharp correction from what were likely higher March peaks reflects the unusual dynamic where war-driven dollar strength is suppressing rather than supporting gold.

### Silver

Silver closed at approximately **$72.97/oz** on April 3, with significant intraday volatility (range: $69.47 - $76.85). One source reported silver slumping 6.9% to $72.48 on the day as de-escalation hopes briefly dented safe-haven demand. Silver remains up **~129% year-over-year** despite recent weakness.

Silver's dual industrial/monetary character makes it more volatile than gold in this environment. Industrial demand signals are mixed — the Iran crisis creates supply chain disruption for industrial metals broadly, but the economic slowdown risk from high energy prices suppresses industrial demand expectations.

### Copper

LME copper was at approximately **$12,138/tonne** as of April 2. Copper appears to have pulled back roughly **3.8%** over the March 5 - April 5 period, reflecting recession/stagflation fears from the energy crisis overriding the structural supply deficit thesis.

**Thesis connection:** The critical minerals thesis projects a 150,000-330,000 tonne copper deficit starting 2026, with J.P. Morgan and ICSG both forecasting shortfalls. Freeport-McMoRan's Grasberg restart (Q2 2026) remains the key near-term supply catalyst. However, the Iran crisis introduces a competing demand-destruction narrative: if high oil prices trigger a global recession, copper demand forecasts would need to be revised downward. At $12,138/tonne, copper is trading above the StoneX 2026 average forecast of $11,490/mt, suggesting some supply-deficit premium remains intact despite macro headwinds.

## Commodities

### Uranium

Uranium spot was at approximately **$84/lb U3O8** as of early April, having pulled back from a January 29 year-to-date high of **$101.41/lb**. The decline from the January peak reflects broader risk-off sentiment and investor reallocation to safe-haven assets, not weakening fundamentals.

**Thesis connection:** The nuclear thesis is strongly reinforced by the Hormuz crisis. The structural supply deficit of 30-40M lbs annually persists. The Iran conflict has amplified energy security concerns, making the case for nuclear baseload power more compelling. Hyperscaler nuclear PPAs ($50B+ contracted with Constellation, Vistra, Talen) are insulated from fossil fuel supply disruption. Cameco (CCJ), Kazatomprom (KAP), and the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust (SRUUF) remain positioned for the structural deficit. Kazatomprom's 2026 production target is ~10% below capacity, maintaining price discipline.

### Lithium & Rare Earths

Lithium carbonate prices vary significantly by region. In February 2026, battery-grade lithium carbonate was at approximately **$17.38/kg in NE Asia** and **$10.21/kg in North America**. Chinese domestic prices have been fluctuating in a **CNY 80,000-150,000/tonne** range. Spot lithium has recovered from the 2024 lows (sub-$10,000/tonne) but remains well below the 2022 peak (~$80,000/tonne).

An emerging dynamic: rare earth shortages are creating a secondary lithium price premium — roughly 15% of current lithium price premiums are attributed to production bottlenecks caused by rare earth component shortages in extraction equipment, not lithium scarcity itself.

**Thesis connection:** China's temporary suspension of rare earth export controls (through November 2026) remains the critical watch item. The November 2026 US-China trade agreement expiry is the single most important catalyst for the critical minerals thesis. The Iran conflict adds another dimension: rare earth and critical mineral supply chains running through the Strait of Hormuz face the same disruption risk as oil.

### Agricultural Commodities

CBOT futures as of April 1:
- **Corn:** 454.25 c/bu (May contract) — vs. USDA 2026 marketing year average projection of $4.20/bu
- **Soybeans:** 1,168.50 c/bu (May contract) — vs. USDA projection of $10.30/bu
- **Wheat:** 597.50 c/bu (May contract) — vs. USDA projection of $5.00/bu

All three crops remain **below estimated break-even levels** ($5.00/bu corn, $12.27/bu soybeans, $7.96/bu wheat), indicating continued margin pressure for producers. The Iran crisis adds risk via fertilizer shortages and costs — the Hormuz closure has disrupted fertilizer supply chains, raising food security concerns particularly in Europe and developing nations.

## Forex

### US Dollar Index (DXY)

DXY closed at **100.22** on Friday April 3, up **0.20%** on the day, edging back above the 100 level after stronger-than-expected US jobs data. Over the past month, the dollar has strengthened **1.47%**, though it remains down **2.72%** over the trailing 12 months.

The dollar is acting as the primary safe-haven asset in the Iran crisis, benefiting from both flight-to-safety flows and the Fed's higher-for-longer stance. This dollar strength is the key driver of gold's counterintuitive weakness and is pressuring emerging market currencies.

### Emerging Market Currencies

**USD/CNY** is forecast in the 6.82 range for April, with China's policy stance focused on stability rather than reflation. The PBoC is unlikely to tolerate sharp or disorderly CNY moves. China's status as a permitted Hormuz transit nation gives it a relative advantage in energy imports, which should support the renminbi versus other EM currencies.

Broader EM FX faces headwinds from dollar strength, higher oil prices (negative for oil importers), and rising recession risk. Oil-exporting EM currencies (MXN, BRL, ZAR) may see partial offset from higher commodity revenues, but the demand destruction narrative limits upside.

## Weekend Developments

**CRITICAL — Multiple material developments on Saturday/Sunday April 4-5:**

1. **Trump issues 48-hour ultimatum to Iran (Sunday April 5):** President Trump posted on Truth Social threatening to bomb Iranian power plants and bridges by Tuesday (April 7) at 8:00 PM ET if the Strait of Hormuz is not reopened. Quote: *"Tuesday will be Power Plant Day, and Bridge Day, all wrapped up in one, in Iran."* He separately told Fox News there is a "good chance" of a deal by Monday but added "if they don't make a deal, I am blowing up everything over there." Iran responded that any strikes on civilian facilities would be met with "more forceful" responses.

2. **Iranian drone strikes on Kuwait (Saturday-Sunday April 4-5):** Drones struck the Kuwait Petroleum Corporation headquarters at the Shuwaikh complex, sparking fires. Two power plants and water desalination facilities were also damaged. Bahrain oil storage tanks were also hit. No casualties reported, but the attacks demonstrate Iran's willingness to expand the conflict to Gulf state energy infrastructure beyond the Hormuz chokepoint. Kuwait oil exports remain halted under force majeure.

3. **OPEC+ April 5 meeting:** The group approved in principle a production increase for May but acknowledged that physical supply is constrained by infrastructure damage and the Hormuz blockade. The increase is symbolic — damaged facilities cannot quickly be brought back online.

4. **Selective Hormuz transits resuming (very limited):** An Iraqi-linked oil tanker was observed transiting the Strait on April 5. Iran and Oman are in diplomatic discussions about "smooth transit." Traffic remains down >90% from normal.

5. **Oil likely to gap higher Monday:** With the Tuesday deadline, Kuwait infrastructure attacks, and continued Hormuz closure, oil futures are expected to open significantly higher when trading resumes. The market must now price the probability of a major US strike on Iranian civilian infrastructure vs. a last-minute diplomatic breakthrough.

**Net assessment:** The situation has escalated materially over the weekend. The Tuesday deadline creates a binary event risk: either a diplomatic breakthrough (bearish oil, bullish risk assets) or infrastructure strikes that could deepen the crisis further (bullish oil, bearish everything else). Markets will reprice sharply Monday morning.
