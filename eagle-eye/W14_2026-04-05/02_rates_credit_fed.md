| 30-Year  | 4.88%       | -10 bps            | 4.97-4.98%         |

**Narrative:** Treasuries rallied meaningfully this week, reversing a significant portion of the March selloff. The 10-year fell 13 bps from 4.44% to 4.31%, pulling back from its July 2025 high, while the 30-year retreated from the psychologically significant 5% level to 4.88%. The 2-year dropped 10 bps to 3.83%, a notable move given that the front end had been anchored by hawkish Fed repricing throughout March.

The rally was driven by two converging forces: (1) diplomatic de-escalation signals on Iran -- reports on April 2 that Iran and Oman were drafting a protocol to "monitor" Strait of Hormuz traffic, plus Trump's comments that Iran's president had asked for a ceasefire, triggered a relief bid in bonds; and (2) the 10-year's intra-week spike to 4.38% on April 1 attracted buyers who saw the selloff as overdone relative to underlying economic fundamentals.

The week was volatile within the range. On April 1, yields initially fell on peace hopes before reversing higher. On April 2, the 10-year touched 4.38% intraday after Trump's national address pledged continued military action against Iran for "several more weeks," before the Oman mediation report pulled yields back to 4.31%. By April 3, the 10-year held at 4.31% as a tug-of-war between inflation fears and recession hedging reached a temporary equilibrium. However, the 4.37% level printed by the housing-focused FinancialContent report on April 3 suggests some intraday volatility persisted.

Despite the weekly rally, yields remain dramatically elevated compared to pre-conflict levels. The 10-year was 3.97% in late February before the Iran strikes began -- 34 bps lower than the current close. The March-to-date repricing has not been meaningfully unwound.

## Yield Curve

| Spread         | Level (4/3) | Direction             | Prior Week (3/27) |
|----------------|-------------|-----------------------|--------------------|
| 2s10s          | +48 bps     | Modest flattening     | +51 bps            |
| 3m10y          | +61 bps     | Positive, narrowing   | +71 bps            |

**Narrative:** The yield curve flattened modestly this week as the long end rallied more than the short end. The 2s10s spread narrowed from +51 bps to approximately +48 bps, while the 3m10y spread compressed from +71 bps to approximately +61 bps (with the 3-month T-bill yield at 3.70% vs the 10-year at 4.31%).

The curve remains positively sloped across all key spreads -- a significant change from the prolonged inversion that lasted from October 2022 through December 2024. The 3m10y spread has been oscillating between positive and negative territory since February 26, reflecting deep uncertainty about the economic outlook.

The flattening dynamic this week is notable because it reflects a mix of drivers: (a) the long end rallied on ceasefire hopes and safe-haven demand from equity weakness; (b) the short end remained anchored by the Fed's hold stance and lack of imminent cut signals. This is a more constructive signal than the bear steepening that dominated March, where the long end was rising faster on inflation fears. However, the NY Fed's recession probability model assigns a 25% chance of recession by November 2026 -- elevated enough to warrant continued curve monitoring.

### Fed Speakers This Week (3/30 - 4/3)
Specific detailed speaker transcripts for this week were not fully captured in available sources. However, the broader tone of post-FOMC Fed communication has been hawkish-cautious:

- **Chair Powell** was scheduled to speak Monday March 30 (per prior week's calendar) -- his first post-March FOMC appearance. At the March presser, Powell pushed back on near-term rate cuts and stated: *"The rate forecast is conditional on the performance of the economy. So if we don't see that progress, then you won't see a rate cut."* He dismissed "stagflation" as too strong a characterization of current conditions.
- **NY Fed President Williams** also spoke Monday evening. Williams has been described as a key "centrist" voice on the FOMC.
- The FOMC blackout period begins April 18 (midnight the second Saturday before the April 28-29 meeting), so speakers remain free to communicate through mid-April.

### Powell Tenure & Warsh Transition
- **Powell's term as Chair expires May 15, 2026.** This is the single most important institutional development in rates markets.
- **Kevin Warsh nominated January 30.** Formally transmitted to Senate March 30. Confirmation hearing scheduled for **April 16** before Senate Banking Committee.
- **Warsh is perceived as hawkish** on inflation and supportive of a "leaner Fed" -- markets view his appointment as potentially more accommodative to the White House but with credible inflation-fighting credentials.
- **Political complications:** Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) is blocking Warsh's nomination until the DOJ's criminal investigation of Powell is dropped. On April 3, a federal judge reaffirmed his decision to block subpoenas in the Powell investigation, calling the DOJ's case baseless ("total lack of a good-faith basis to suspect a crime"). An appeal by the DOJ is expected. Sen. Warren (D-MA) has attacked Warsh as a "rubber stamp for Trump."
- **Timeline risk:** With the hearing on April 16, committee vote, and Senate floor vote needing 2-6 weeks, confirmation before Powell's May 15 departure is possible but tight. A gap in Fed leadership would create significant market uncertainty.

**Narrative:** Credit spreads showed "early signs of widening" this week, according to Morningstar, as the Iran war's inflationary shock works its way through credit markets. IG OAS held near 120 bps -- roughly flat with the prior week and still below the long-term average of ~130-150 bps, but meaningfully wider than the sub-100 bps tights of early 2025. HY OAS at approximately 316-346 bps (various sources show the range) has widened 30 bps YTD and 40 bps from YTD lows.

Morningstar and Lord Abbett both flagged that if oil prices remain above $110/bbl, HY spreads could widen to 450-500 bps. The bifurcation by credit quality persists: BB-rated credits remain resilient at ~199 bps while CCC & Lower trades near 974 bps, reflecting concentrated stress in the most leveraged and energy-sensitive segments.

**Private credit stress is building.** Morgan Stanley expects 8% annual default rates in private credit between H2 2026 and H1 2027, driven by AI disruption of software companies that are heavily concentrated in private credit portfolios. CNBC reported that private credit's "zero-loss fantasy is coming to an end" as defaults and fund exits rise. While analysts describe this as "significant but not systemic," the simultaneous pressure from defaults, redemptions, and mark-to-market dispersion is notable.

**IG issuance:** Primary markets remain active. Full-year 2026 forecasts call for up to $2.25T in IG issuance (+35% YoY), though rising yields may slow the pace for lower-rated borrowers. Schwab recommends an "up-in-quality" posture given tight spreads relative to risk.

## Liquidity

### Fed Balance Sheet & Reserve Management
- **QT status:** Ended December 1, 2025. Reserve Management Purchases (RMPs) of ~$40B/month in T-bills continue. The Fed had planned to "significantly reduce" RMPs after Tax Day (mid-April) -- this upcoming taper is a key watch item.
- **Reserve balances:** ~$3.02T as of late March. Considered "ample" but lacking the excess cushion of 2022-2023.
- **MBS rolloff:** Continuing passively, converting the portfolio toward all-Treasuries.

### Treasury General Account (TGA)
- **Balance:** ~$871B (as of March 20, 2026). The TGA is elevated as Treasury rebuilds cash buffers post-debt-ceiling. This level acts as a moderate liquidity drain from the banking system.

### Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP)
- **Balance:** Near zero (~$10-20B range), effectively drained.
- The ON RRP peaked above $2.5T in mid-2023 and has been drawn down as T-bill yields exceed the ON RRP rate (3.50%). Money market funds prefer higher-yielding bills over parking cash at the Fed.
- **Critical implication:** With the ON RRP buffer gone, the Fed's RMPs are the sole mechanism preventing reserve scarcity. The upcoming taper of RMPs after mid-April creates a potential friction point, particularly if quarter-end and tax-season cash flows tighten liquidity.

### Liquidity Assessment
Liquidity conditions remain adequate but structurally fragile. The $3.02T in reserves is sufficient, and the RMPs provide ongoing support. However, three risks are converging in mid-to-late April: (1) the planned RMP taper; (2) tax-day cash flows draining reserves into the TGA; and (3) continued bond market volatility (the MOVE index was above 130 in late March). The Liberty Street Economics blog from the NY Fed published a timely analysis on April 1 examining "Treasury Market Liquidity Since April 2025," acknowledging that market functioning metrics have deteriorated relative to the pre-conflict period. The Fed's standing repo facility (SRF) remains available as a backstop, but its use at year-end 2025 ($75B) and subsequent drop to zero highlights the episodic nature of liquidity stress.

## Weekend Developments (Post-Friday 4/3 Close)

### Iran Escalation Intensifies Over the Weekend
The most significant weekend development is a major escalation in the Iran conflict. On Sunday April 5, President Trump warned Iran in an "expletive-filled social media post" that the country would be "living in hell" if the Strait of Hormuz is not opened by Tuesday. He threatened to bomb Iran's power plants and bridges. Oil prices surged over the weekend: WTI crude jumped 2.35% to $114.16/bbl and Brent advanced 1.72% to $110.91/bbl. The average price of US gasoline passed $4/gallon for the first time since 2022.

This dramatically escalatory rhetoric -- coming after a mid-week hint at ceasefire talks -- reintroduces maximum uncertainty into rates markets heading into the April 7 open. Bond traders face a dilemma: safe-haven demand for Treasuries competes with inflation fears from persistently elevated oil. Throughout March, inflation fears won (yields rose). The question for this coming week is whether Trump's Tuesday ultimatum triggers a capitulation by Iran (bullish for bonds) or a further escalation (bearish on inflation expectations, mixed for bonds depending on the growth/inflation balance).

### Warsh Hearing Confirmed for April 16
The Senate Banking Committee confirmed Friday (April 4) that Kevin Warsh's confirmation hearing will be held April 16. This is the most important institutional event for rates markets this month, as it determines whether the Fed leadership transition happens smoothly before Powell's May 15 departure. Tillis's hold and the DOJ appeal of the blocked subpoenas add uncertainty.

### March FOMC Minutes Due April 8
The minutes from the March 17-18 meeting will be released Wednesday April 8. Markets will scrutinize the internal debate about oil-driven inflation persistence and any discussion of rate-hike contingency planning. Given the 7-7 split on the dot plot (no cuts vs. one cut), the minutes may reveal which camp is gaining ground.

### No Emergency Fed Actions
No emergency FOMC meetings, rate changes, or extraordinary liquidity interventions announced through Saturday evening.
