# Rates, Credit & Fed — Week Ending 2026-05-15

This was a watershed week for fixed income: a hot CPI/PPI combo, weak Treasury auctions, the Senate's 54-45 confirmation of Kevin Warsh as the next Fed Chair, Powell's final days at the helm, and an oil shock from the ongoing Strait of Hormuz crisis combined to drive a sharp bear-steepening of the curve. The 10Y closed Friday at 4.59% and the 30Y at 5.12% — both the highest levels since early 2025/late 2023 respectively.

The week saw a relentless bear-steepening drive. Yields climbed gradually Monday-Thursday before accelerating Friday as the 10Y surged ~14 bp on the day and the 30Y jumped ~11 bp on the day to close at its highest level since 2007. CNBC and Wolf Street confirmed the 30Y print at 5.121%/5.127% with the 10Y at 4.595%. Three primary drivers: (1) a hot April CPI release (3.8% headline YoY, highest since May 2023; 2.8% core, both above expectations), (2) a PPI shock with headline +1.4% MoM (vs +0.5% expected) and services PPI blowing out for a 6.0% YoY print, and (3) Cleveland Fed's May CPI nowcast pointing to 4.2% YoY — what would be the highest since April 2023.

The Treasury supply pressure compounded the inflation story. The Treasury sold $691B of securities this week, including a $25B 30Y auction Wednesday that tailed with a 5.046% high yield and 2.30 bid-to-cover (below the 2.40 trailing average). The $58B 3Y and $42B 10Y auctions also drew below-average demand.

## Yield Curve

The 2s10s closed near +50 bp on May 15 per ETFdb's snapshot, a meaningful bear-steepener from prior weeks. ⚠️ Specific weekly change in 2s10s not directly confirmed in sources, but with the 30Y up ~25 bp and the 2Y up ~10 bp, the long end clearly outpaced the front end. The 3m10y was not directly located but should be deeply positive given Fed funds at 3.50-3.75% and the 10Y at 4.59%. The NY Fed yield-curve recession model has 12-month US recession odds at ~21% — relatively low and consistent with the term-premium-driven (not Fed-tightening-driven) nature of this move. The bear-steepening signature is characteristic of an inflation/supply scare rather than a growth scare.

## Fed Watch

**Pricing**: Per CME FedWatch (May 6), markets assigned a 95.9% probability the Fed holds at 3.50-3.75% at the June 16-17 meeting. Following Friday's inflation/yield blowout, markets are now pricing a near-zero chance of any cuts in 2026 and a roughly 50% probability of a hike by December — a dramatic hawkish repricing of the path. The implied terminal rate has shifted higher; Saxo flagged that Warsh inherits a setup where markets currently see "no changes to rates this year."

**Leadership transition**: The Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as the 17th Fed Chair on Wednesday May 13, 2026 in a 54-45 vote — the most divisive Fed-chair confirmation in history. Powell's term ended Friday May 15. Powell will remain on the Board of Governors, citing "a series of legal attacks on the Fed which threaten our ability to conduct monetary policy without considering political factors." His final FOMC presser was at the April 28-29 meeting.

**April 28-29 FOMC**: Held rates at 3.50-3.75% with FOUR dissents — the most since October 1992. Governor Stephen Miran dissented dovishly (preferred a 25 bp cut). Cleveland's Hammack, Minneapolis's Kashkari, and Dallas's Logan dissented hawkishly, opposing language in the statement suggesting the next move would be a cut. ⚠️ FOMC minutes for the April 28-29 meeting would have been due roughly Wednesday May 20, after the report period — not yet released as of May 17.

**Warsh's stance**: Structurally hawkish but has signaled support for some rate cuts on AI-driven disinflation. First FOMC under his chairmanship will be June 16-17. No formal Warsh speeches occurred in the May 11-15 window; he was navigating confirmation and transition.

The earlier 2026 narrative — Feb-April widening of ~100 bp on HY driven by tariff and Iran-war fears — has unwound, with spreads tightening through May despite the surge in nominal yields. No fresh stress signals located in available sources for the May 11-15 window. The story is rates-led, not credit-led.

## Liquidity

- **TGA**: Treasury General Account at ~$905 billion (recent reading), elevated and absorbing reserves. The week's $691B in gross issuance partly funds this elevated cash buffer alongside coupon settlements.
- **ON RRP**: Has effectively collapsed to ~$2 billion — functionally empty. The ON RRP buffer that previously insulated reserves from TGA fluctuations is no longer operative. Any further TGA build now comes directly out of bank reserves, tightening interbank liquidity.
- **Implication**: With ON RRP drained, the liquidity buffer to absorb the $691B weekly issuance and TGA refills is gone. This was a contributing factor to the weak auctions and the term-premium spike. Wolf Street and Fortune both flagged "the bond market is shouting" about supply/demand strain.

## Weekend Developments

This weekend's developments are material and ongoing:

1. **Strait of Hormuz crisis continues**: Shipping through the strait has been largely blocked since Feb 28, 2026 when the US and Israel launched the air war against Iran. Brent crude closed Friday at $109.26/bbl, WTI at $105.42, with oil up >80% YTD and +9% on the week. CNBC published a Saturday May 16 piece flagging "the bond market is flashing a warning over Iran" — energy-geopolitics-driven inflation concerns are dominating the bond complex.

2. **G7 Finance Ministers meeting Monday-Tuesday in Paris**: Finance ministers and central bankers convene to address the economic consequences of the prolonged Hormuz closure. This is a tail risk for Monday's open — coordinated statements could move rates either direction.

3. **Warsh-era opens**: First weekend with Warsh as Fed Chair (since Powell's term ended at end of Friday). Fortune published a Sunday May 17 piece on "red and green flags" Wall Street is watching as the Warsh era begins. No Warsh weekend speech located, but his maiden statement could come at any time.

4. **Cleveland Fed May CPI nowcast at 4.2% YoY**: Released within the past week and influencing weekend rates positioning. If realized, this would mark a clean break above 4% headline — a second wave of inflation narrative that's now embedded in pricing.
