## Treasury Yields

The week ending March 6 was defined by a violent tug-of-war between safe-haven demand (weak jobs data, recession fears) and inflation repricing (oil shock from the Iran/Strait of Hormuz crisis). The inflation channel won on the long end, with 10Y and 30Y yields surging, while the short end rallied on flight-to-safety and rate-cut expectations. The result: dramatic curve steepening.

| Maturity | Close (Mar 6) | Prior Week Close (Feb 27) | Weekly Change (bp) | Notes |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|-------------------|-------|
| **2-Year** | 3.56% | 3.38% | +18 bp | Rebounded from 4-month low; still well below January highs |
| **5-Year** | ~3.72% | ~3.55% (est.) | ~+17 bp | Estimated from available data; caught between front-end safety bid and back-end inflation repricing |

| **30-Year** | 4.77% | ~4.60% (est.) | ~+17 bp | Back to April 2024 levels -- erasing the entire benefit of the Fed's rate-cutting cycle |

**Key context:** The 10Y flipped wildly during the week. On Monday March 2, it plunged to 3.93% on recession fears before spiking 14 bp in a single session to 4.07% as oil surged. By Thursday it hit 4.13% on the oil shock, and Friday's weak payrolls initially sent it lower before closing at 4.15% as inflation fears dominated. The 30Y at 4.77% means 30-year fixed mortgage rates jumped to 6.14% (from 5.99% the prior week), reversing the housing affordability gains of recent months.

## Yield Curve

| Spread | Level (Mar 6) | Prior Week (Feb 27) | Weekly Change | Status |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|--------------|--------|
| **2s10s** | +59 bp | +59 bp | ~Flat | Positive (normal); steepened dramatically intra-week |
| **3m10y** | +33-37 bp | ~+15-20 bp (est.) | ~+15 bp wider | Positive -- NO INVERSION |

**Shape analysis:** The yield curve is in a classic **bear steepening** regime -- the long end is selling off faster than the short end. This is the textbook "inflation scare" shape: the market expects the Fed to hold or cut (anchoring the short end) while pricing in higher inflation and term premium on the long end.

The 2s10s spread has been positive since late 2024, meaning the historic inversion that began in mid-2022 has fully unwound. The 3m10y spread at +33-37 bp confirms no inversion is present. However, the curve's steepening is being driven by the *wrong* reasons -- not growth optimism, but stagflation fears. This is the most dangerous configuration for risk assets.

**Recession signal update:** The 3m10y spread briefly re-inverted in late 2025 but has since normalized. The traditional 12-month recession indicator from a sustained inversion period has not triggered for the current period. However, the labor market deterioration (unemployment at 4.4%, -92K payrolls) suggests recession risk is elevated regardless of curve shape.

## Fed Watch

### FOMC Meeting Pricing (March 18, 2026 -- SEP Meeting)

| Outcome | Probability |
|---------|------------|
| **Hold at 3.50-3.75%** | **94.1%** |
| Cut 25 bp to 3.25-3.50% | 5.9% |

The March meeting is effectively a done deal -- the Fed will hold. This is a SEP meeting (Summary of Economic Projections with dot plot), making it critical for forward guidance. The market will scrutinize:
- Whether the median dot still shows cuts in 2026 or shifts to a prolonged hold
- How the SEP incorporates the oil shock and tariff effects on inflation projections
- Any language acknowledging stagflation risks

### Year-End 2026 Pricing

At the start of 2026, markets priced 3 rate cuts (75 bp) by year-end. As of March 6, that has collapsed to approximately **1 cut (25 bp)** by year-end, with some instruments pricing zero cuts. The oil shock and sticky inflation have dramatically repriced the path. Swaps markets show a 97% probability of hold at the March FOMC.

### January FOMC Minutes (Released Feb 18) -- Key Revelations

The January 27-28 meeting minutes were the most hawkish in the current cycle:

- **Several officials pointed to a rate-HIKE scenario** if inflation remains stubbornly high -- a dramatic shift from the three consecutive cuts in late 2025
- The committee described the hold as a "hawkish pause" with the door open to tightening
- **Two dissents**: Governors Miran and Waller voted for a 25 bp cut, favoring a faster path to neutral
- Core PCE running at **2.9%** -- well above the 2% target -- was cited as the primary reason for caution
- The minutes stated that if core PCE shows "any sign of accelerating," the committee's "two-sided" language could become a reality, potentially leading to a rate increase

### Warsh Nomination & Fed Leadership Transition

- **March 4**: Trump officially nominated Kevin Warsh as Fed Chair to succeed Powell (whose term expires May 15, 2026)
- Warsh is perceived as relatively hawkish and a "sound money" advocate, signaling an aggressive balance sheet pivot and potential rate normalization
- **Complication**: Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) is blocking the nomination in the Senate until a federal criminal investigation of Powell by the D.C. U.S. attorney's office is dropped
- Confirmation hearings expected throughout March, but timeline uncertain
- Markets are pricing Warsh as more hawkish than Powell on balance sheet management, but potentially more pragmatic on rates given his Wall Street background

### Fed Speakers -- Key Commentary This Week

- **Vice Chair Michelle Bowman (March 3)**: Began the process with Treasury Secretary Bessent of reassessing and reforming bank liquidity requirements, aiming to enable banks to "make more loans to businesses and households, rather than the government." Hawkish on inflation, advocating continued caution on cuts.
- **Fed blackout period**: The FOMC blackout period for the March 17-18 meeting begins Saturday March 7, meaning no more Fed speeches until after the decision.

### Inflation Backdrop

The stagflation cocktail is now the dominant narrative:
- Core PCE: 2.9% (well above 2% target)
- Oil shock: Brent at $93+ on Friday, crossing $100 over the weekend
- Tariffs: 15% universal tariff imposed Feb 24 under Section 122; China tariffs increased another 10% on March 4
- Average hourly earnings: +3.8% YoY (above expectations)
- These inputs make the March SEP inflation projections the single most important data point of the meeting

### Spread Levels

| Index | Level (est. Mar 6) | Trend | Historical Context |
|-------|-------------------|-------|-------------------|
| **IG OAS** (ICE BofA US Corporate) | ~80-85 bp | Widening from Jan tights of 71 bp | Near 30-year tights (71 bp in late Jan); beginning to widen |
| **HY OAS** (ICE BofA US HY) | ~300 bp | Stable but bifurcated | Below long-term average; tightest levels in 20 years for BB |
| **CCC & Lower** | Elevated | Widening | Diverging meaningfully from BB/B; stress visible in lowest tier |
| **BBB** | ~100-110 bp | Slight widening | Closest to IG/HY boundary; most rate-sensitive |

### Key Credit Market Dynamics

**Spread bifurcation is the story.** BB-rated bonds remain near historic tights, while CCC-and-below spreads have widened materially and are approaching their 20-year averages. This two-tier structure signals the market is differentiating between quality credits and stressed names, rather than repricing credit risk broadly.

**Supply pressure mounting.** 2026 high-grade bond issuance is forecast at $2.25 trillion -- a 35% YoY increase. For the first time since 2022, supply may meaningfully outpace demand, creating technical pressure for wider spreads even absent a fundamental deterioration.

**Oil shock implications for credit.** The Strait of Hormuz crisis creates asymmetric credit risk:
- **Energy sector**: Positive for E&P and oilfield services credit quality; negative for airlines, transportation, and energy-intensive manufacturers
- **Airlines & transport**: Already thin margins face cost pressure from $90+ crude
- **Retail/consumer**: Gasoline price surge ($3.00 to $3.45 and rising) squeezes consumer discretionary spending, stressing lower-quality consumer-facing credits

**Default outlook.** Default rates remain subdued but are expected to tick higher. Banks in the January SLOOS reported expecting loan quality to deteriorate for C&I loans to small firms and for most consumer loan categories. High-profile defaults in late 2025 are a leading indicator.

### Senior Loan Officer Survey (January 2026)

Key findings from the most recent SLOOS:
- Banks reported **tighter lending standards** for C&I loans to firms of all sizes
- **Weaker demand** for auto loans and other consumer loans
- Banks expect loan quality to **deteriorate for small-firm C&I loans** and most consumer categories
- Banks expect CRE loan quality to **improve** (a notable divergence)
- Banks reported being **more likely to approve loans to firms benefiting from AI** and less likely for firms adversely affected by AI -- a new structural lending dynamic
- Demand expected to strengthen across categories, but standards to remain unchanged

## Liquidity

### Federal Reserve Balance Sheet

| Metric | Level | Change |
|--------|-------|--------|
| **Total Fed Assets** | ~$6.5-6.6T | Flat since QT ended Dec 1, 2025 |
| **Reserve Balances** | $3.02T | +$83B since RMPs began in December |
| **QT Status** | **ENDED** (Dec 1, 2025) | Balance sheet reduced from $8.9T peak to $6.5T |

**Reserve Management Purchases (RMPs):** Since QT ended, the Fed has been conducting RMPs -- purchasing Treasury bills to replace maturing agency MBS, keeping the balance sheet flat while shifting composition toward Treasuries. Reserve balances have risen $83 billion since this program started, to $3.02 trillion.

### Treasury General Account (TGA)

| Metric | Level |
|--------|-------|
| **TGA Balance** (Mar 3) | ~$860B |

The TGA at $860 billion is elevated, reflecting tax receipts and ongoing issuance. Drawdowns from this level would inject liquidity into the system; rebuilds would drain it. With the debt ceiling suspended (following the 2025 resolution), the Treasury has flexibility in managing this balance.

### Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP)

| Metric | Level |
|--------|-------|
| **ON RRP Usage** | ~$5-10B (effectively zero) |
| **Peak (2023)** | $2.5T |
| **Decline** | -99.8% |

**The ON RRP is functionally drained.** From a peak of $2.5 trillion in 2023, the facility has been reduced to single-digit billions. This has critical implications:

- **The liquidity shock absorber is gone.** For two years, the ON RRP absorbed surplus cash and shielded bank reserves from volatility. With the buffer at zero, every marginal liquidity shock -- heavy Treasury issuance, quarter-end repositioning, foreign capital flows -- falls directly on reserves.
- **Short-term funding more fragile.** Year-end 2025 saw the Standing Repo Facility (SRF) spike to $75 billion before collapsing back to zero in early January, demonstrating the system's vulnerability to liquidity stress events.
- **This is why the Fed ended QT.** The depletion of ON RRP was the primary signal that reserves had declined to their "efficient and effective" level, triggering the end of balance sheet runoff.

### Net Liquidity Assessment

The liquidity backdrop is adequate but fragile:
- Reserve balances at $3.02T are "ample" by the Fed's definition
- QT has ended, removing the steady drain
- But the ON RRP buffer at zero means there is no cushion for unexpected liquidity demands
- The TGA at $860B provides some countercyclical capacity if drawn down
- Key risk: a sustained period of heavy Treasury issuance combined with the oil shock could create funding market stress, particularly at quarter-end (March 31)

## Weekend Developments

### CRITICAL: Iran War / Strait of Hormuz Crisis Escalation

This is the dominant macro development and will drive Monday's open:

- **Oil breached $100/barrel over the weekend.** Brent traded at $101.81 and WTI at $101.56 on Sunday evening -- the first time oil has hit triple digits since the 2022 Russia/Ukraine crisis.
- **Weekly oil price surge was the largest in history.** WTI soared 35.63% for the week -- the biggest weekly gain in the history of the futures contract dating back to 1983.
- **Strait of Hormuz effectively closed.** Tanker traffic dropped to approximately zero after IRGC warnings and attacks on vessels. Over 150 ships anchored outside the strait. Maersk suspended two services linking the Middle East to Asia and Europe.
- **Qatar LNG facility shut down** after Iranian drone attack, compounding natural gas supply concerns for Asia and Europe.
- **Iraq shutting oil production** because it has nowhere to export through the strait.
- **QatarEnergy Minister warned** oil could hit $150/barrel and "bring down economies of the world" if the war lasts weeks.
- **Trump administration response:** $20B reinsurance program for tankers announced Friday; U.S. Energy Secretary Wright said disruption would last "weeks, certainly not months."
- **U.S. gasoline:** Average regular price surged from ~$3.00 to $3.45 and rising.

**Rates implication:** This is massively inflationary in the near term. Expect further bear steepening on Monday. The 10Y could test 4.25-4.30% and the 30Y could approach 4.90-5.00% if the crisis deepens. The 2Y may rally modestly on safe-haven demand, but the inflation pass-through will eventually pressure the short end too. The March 18 FOMC meeting now has a radically different inflation backdrop than what was expected even two weeks ago.

### Warsh Nomination Formally Sent to Senate (March 4)

The formal nomination creates a concrete timeline for Senate Banking Committee hearings in March. The Tillis blockade adds uncertainty. Markets will price in the "Warsh premium" -- expectations of a more hawkish balance sheet approach and potentially faster normalization.

### Bowman-Bessent Liquidity Reform Initiative

Vice Chair Bowman and Treasury Secretary Bessent jointly began reassessing bank liquidity requirements this week. The goal is to enable banks to "make more loans to businesses and households rather than the government." This could eventually loosen SLR and LCR constraints, freeing bank balance sheet capacity to intermediate Treasury markets. Positive for liquidity long-term, but implementation is months/years away.

### Kraken Master Account Approval

The Fed's Kansas City branch approved a "limited purpose" master account for crypto firm Kraken (a Wyoming SPDI). While not directly rates-relevant, it signals continued institutional integration of crypto into the traditional financial system and has drawn criticism from the Bank Policy Institute.

### February Jobs Report Aftermath

The -92K payroll print (vs. +50K expected) was the third negative month in the past five. Key details:
- Healthcare lost 28K (Kaiser Permanente strike in CA/HI)
- Federal government employment fell 10K
- Unemployment rate: 4.4%
- Average duration of unemployment: 25.7 weeks (longest since December 2021)
- Average hourly earnings: +0.4% MoM, +3.8% YoY (both above expectations)

The combination of weak employment + hot wages + surging oil = textbook stagflation signal. This is the worst possible data configuration for the Fed, as it simultaneously argues for both rate cuts (weak labor market) and rate holds/hikes (hot inflation). The market response -- yields rising on the long end despite weak jobs data -- confirms that inflation fears are dominating growth fears.
