## Energy

### Crude Oil

The week was dominated by the US-Iran ceasefire (announced April 8) and its subsequent unraveling. Oil posted massive intra-week swings before collapsing on ceasefire optimism, only to face a weekend reversal as talks failed.

| Benchmark | Friday Close (Apr 10) | Weekly Change | Sunday Proxy (Apr 12) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| WTI (CLK26) | $95.63/bbl | -12% | ~$104 (+8% from Fri) |
| Brent (Jun) | $95.20/bbl | -10% | ~$102 (+8% from Fri) |

**Weekly narrative:** Brent opened the week near $111.25 (Monday). The April 8 US-Iran ceasefire announcement triggered a massive selloff -- Brent crashed 13% to $94.80, WTI plunged over 15%. Markets spent Thursday-Friday consolidating around $95-96 as traders awaited the Islamabad face-to-face talks. Heavy futures liquidation ahead of Saturday's negotiations drove a further 1.3% decline into Friday's close.

**Geopolitical context:** The 2026 Iran war (launched Feb 28) caused the largest oil supply disruption in history. Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz choked off 12-15 million barrels/day of crude flow. Saudi production capacity was reduced 600,000 bpd from Iranian strikes on Manifa and Khurais facilities; the East-West Pipeline lost 700,000 bpd throughput. Even at Friday's "ceasefire-discounted" levels, prices remain ~35% above the pre-war ~$70 baseline.

**OPEC+:** On April 5, OPEC+ agreed to a modest 206,000 bpd increase for May -- less than 2% of the supply disrupted by the Hormuz closure. The symbolic gesture underscores the cartel's inability to offset the shortfall, especially with Gulf infrastructure damage requiring months to repair.

**Inventories:** EIA reported a 3.081M barrel build (week ending Apr 3) vs. expectations for a 1.0M draw. This was the fourth consecutive weekly build, as reduced refinery runs and SPR releases partially offset import disruptions.

## Weekend Developments

### CRITICAL: US-Iran Peace Talks Collapse (April 11-12)

This is the most significant market-moving event since the war began on February 28.

**Timeline:**
- **Saturday Apr 11:** VP Vance met Iranian delegation in Islamabad for face-to-face negotiations mediated by Pakistan. Talks lasted 21 hours.
- **Saturday evening:** Vance announced "we have not reached an agreement." Negotiations broke down over Iran's refusal to accept terms on nuclear weapons development.
- **Sunday Apr 12:** Trump announced on Truth Social that the US Navy will "immediately begin the process of BLOCKADING any and all Ships trying to enter, or leave, the Strait of Hormuz." The blockade targets vessels that have paid tolls to Iran (Iran had been charging >$1M per ship for transit).

**Market implications:**
- **Oil:** Sunday proxy markets show Brent surging ~8% to ~$102, WTI jumping to ~$104. The Friday ceasefire discount is fully reversed. A sustained blockade could push prices back toward the $120 levels seen in early March.
- **Gold:** Expected to benefit from renewed safe-haven flows. Weekend Indian market data shows gold stabilizing near $4,749-4,751.
- **Dollar:** The blockade announcement is ambiguous for USD -- it increases geopolitical risk (dollar positive) but also raises inflation expectations (dollar negative via rate-cut pricing).
- **Equities:** Expect a risk-off open Monday. Energy stocks will outperform; airlines, consumer discretionary, and EM equities will underperform.

### March CPI Report (Released Friday April 10)

US CPI came in at +0.9% MoM / 3.3% YoY -- the hottest monthly reading since mid-2022. Gasoline prices surged 21.2%, accounting for three-quarters of the monthly increase. Core services remained benign at +0.2% MoM. The market is treating this as "transitory" energy inflation, maintaining rate-cut expectations. If the Hormuz blockade persists, April CPI will be even hotter.

### Easter Monday (April 13)

London, Hong Kong, and several European exchanges closed for Easter Monday. Reduced liquidity will amplify any moves driven by the Hormuz blockade news.
