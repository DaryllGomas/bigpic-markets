## Fed Watch

### FOMC Minutes Release (April 8) -- THE WEEK'S HEADLINE

The March 17-18 FOMC minutes were released Wednesday and contained a **hawkish surprise**: multiple participants discussed the possibility that **upward adjustments to the fed funds rate could be appropriate** if inflation remained above target. This is the first explicit mention of potential rate hikes since the current cycle began.

**The 11 words that rattled markets**: *"inflation could prove to be more persistent than the staff anticipated"* -- a direct acknowledgment that the Fed's own models may be underestimating the inflation problem.

Key details from the minutes:
- **Rate held at 3.50-3.75%** (unanimous)
- **7 of 19 members project zero cuts in 2026** (up from the median of one cut in the SEP)
- Some participants argued for a **two-sided description** of policy risks -- i.e., explicitly noting hike risk, not just cut timing
- Core goods inflation was "well above the pace consistent with 2% inflation," which staff "largely attributed to the effects of higher tariffs"
- Near-term inflation expectations had risen due to Middle East energy disruption
- Staff viewed uncertainty as "elevated" given Middle East developments, government policy changes, and AI adoption

**Verdict**: The March minutes mark a clear **hawkish pivot** in Fed communication. The bar for cuts has risen significantly, and for the first time in the cycle, rate hikes are being actively discussed by a vocal contingent.

- **Quantitative tightening officially ended December 1, 2025**
- The Fed is expected to begin **reserve management purchases (RMPs)** in coming months to maintain adequate reserve levels without affecting longer-term borrowing costs
- Post-QT, the key dynamic is reserve redistribution -- the Fed is managing the transition cautiously to avoid a repeat of September 2019 repo market stress

### Bank Lending Conditions
- The **January 2026 SLOOS** (most recent) showed modest tightening of C&I lending standards to firms of all sizes during Q4 2025
- Banks cited **uncertain economic outlook, expected collateral deterioration, and reduced risk tolerance** as drivers
- Demand for C&I loans to large/middle-market firms strengthened, while small firm demand was flat
- Banks expect standards to remain **basically unchanged** through 2026 for most categories, with modest tightening for construction/land development loans
- The next SLOOS release (covering Q1 2026) will be critical -- it should capture the impact of the Iran conflict on lending sentiment

### Liquidity Assessment
Overall liquidity conditions remain **adequate but watchful**. The end of QT removed the primary drain on reserves, and the well-funded TGA provides fiscal cushion. However, the Iran conflict introduces risk of **sudden liquidity demand** if oil price volatility triggers margin calls across energy derivatives. The April 17 options expiration (next week) adds a positioning overlay.

**Timeline:**

- **Oil**: WTI and Brent already jumped ~7% on Saturday electronic trading; Brent approaching $103. If the blockade is enforced, SPR drawdowns are approaching limits (4.5-5M bbl/day shortfall already)
- **Treasury yields**: Expect a **flight-to-quality bid** in the front end and belly, pushing 2Y and 10Y yields lower. But the long end may **sell off** on renewed inflation fears, steepening the curve further.
- **Credit**: HY spreads likely to gap wider, potentially erasing the entire week's 29 bp compression. Energy credits will be whipsawed -- producers benefit from higher oil but face operational disruption.
- **Fed implications**: The collapse eliminates the "transitory energy shock" narrative. If oil stays above $100 through Q2, headline CPI could remain at 3%+ through June, making September rate cuts nearly impossible and hike probability meaningfully higher.

### CPI Aftermath
Friday's March CPI (headline 3.3% YoY, monthly +0.9%) is still being digested. The silver lining was core CPI at 2.6% YoY (below expectations), suggesting underlying inflation is contained. But with the ceasefire now collapsed, the "energy was transitory" argument is dead.
